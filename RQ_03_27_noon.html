College Class Report: Discussion on John 10-13

Synopsis

This class session, held on March 27th, began with a brief centering meditation led by Brad. Attendance was taken, establishing Group D (Olat, Luca, Matt, Mary, Cameron, Stella, Vanya, Serena) as the discussion leaders for the day, with Cameron taking notes. The leaders opted for a Jenga game as their introductory activity instead of Jeopardy. The main focus of the class was a detailed discussion of the Gospel of John, chapters 10 through 13, often referred to as the "hinge" of the gospel. Students explored various themes, including the "I am" statements ("I am the gate," "I am the good shepherd"), the nature of Jesus's relationship with the Father ("The Father and I are one"), the concept of non-dualism (comparing it briefly to Brahman/Ātman), the significance of the shepherd metaphor (self-sacrifice, exclusivity vs. inclusivity, potential power dynamics), the foreshadowing of Jesus's death (grain of wheat metaphor, laying down life), the role of belief and unbelief (hearing thunder vs. God's voice), the anointing by Mary and Judas's reaction, the raising of Lazarus as the seventh sign (and the significance of the fourth day), and the tension between Jesus's displays of humility (washing feet, riding a donkey) and his divine authority. The session concluded with Brad highlighting the completion of the seven signs in John and providing instructions for the next podcast assignment, differentiating tasks based on student groups (Group D to cover the Bhagavad Gītā, others to cover John).

Vocabulary

  • Brahman (ब्राह्मण): The ultimate reality or ground of being in Hinduism, discussed in comparison to the concept of oneness in John.
  • Ātman (आत्मन्): The individual self or soul in Hinduism, discussed in the context of non-dualism (Ātman is Brahman).
  • I am (Greek: ἐγώ εἰμι - egō eimi): Refers to the significant self-declarations of Jesus in the Gospel of John (e.g., "I am the gate," "I am the good shepherd"). While the Greek was not explicitly stated in class, it is the underlying phrase for these key statements.
  • Christ (Greek: Χριστός - Christos): Meaning "anointed one." Discussed in relation to the anointing of Jesus by Mary.
  • Eucharist: The Christian ceremony commemorating the Last Supper, in which bread and wine are consecrated and consumed. Mentioned as being less emphasized in John compared to the foot washing.

Key Takeaways

  • John 10-13 represents a pivotal section ("hinge") in the Gospel of John, marking a shift towards Jesus's passion.
  • The "I am" statements ("the gate," "the good shepherd") offer rich metaphors for Jesus's role, emphasizing guidance, protection, self-sacrifice, and exclusive access to salvation, though also raising questions about interpretation (contradiction, power dynamics).
  • The phrase "The Father and I are one" (John 10:30) suggests a deep unity, prompting comparisons to non-dualistic concepts like Brahman/Ātman, but with the distinct element of Jesus as a mediator.
  • There's a tension between Jesus's divine power/knowledge (knowing the future, knowing who belongs to his flock) and concepts of free will or agency (Judas's choice, predetermined belonging).
  • The concept of belief is central; some perceive divine acts as miracles or messages, while others dismiss them (e.g., hearing God's voice as thunder). Unbelief is presented almost as a state of being ("you do not believe because you do not belong to my sheep").
  • Jesus's actions often contrast societal expectations (riding a donkey vs. a warhorse, washing feet) to emphasize humility, though some students questioned if these actions were performative given his overall authoritative tone.
  • The raising of Lazarus is the seventh and final "sign" in John, highlighting Jesus's power over death, emphasized by the intentional waiting until the fourth day (past the Jewish belief of the spirit lingering for three days).
  • Sensory details are important for belief: seeing, smelling (Lazarus's tomb, the anointing oil) reinforce the reality of events.
  • John's Gospel emphasizes the foot washing (John 13) over the institution of the Eucharist found in other gospels, highlighting service and humility.
