Course Description & Objectives

Course Description

The Religious Quest: Creation, Creativity, and ReCreation in Hindu and Christian Sacred Texts invites students to explore fundamental human questions through sacred scriptures, such as: Who am I? What is this world I find myself within? What is my purpose? By examining creation myths, epistemologies, ontologies, and ethical teachings from both Hindu and Christian traditions, this course fosters dialogue between diverse perspectives. Anchored in Sruti and Smriti on Tuesdays and Hebrew and Greek Testaments on Thursdays, students will closely examine the Fourth Gospel and the Bhagavad-Gita (भगवद्गीता) to engage with themes of divine play (लीला), non-dualism (अद्वैत), and ethical responsibility (धर्म). Integrating ecological, feminist, and liberation theologies, the course challenges participants to view sacred texts not merely as ancient records but as dynamic resources for meaning-making in a pluralistic world. Together, we will faithfully seek to understand the diversely creative ways that humans who identify as Hindu and Christian make meaning, value creation, respond to injustice, and playfully (re)create a sustainable future.

Satisfies Core requirement for: Theology (Sacred Texts and Traditions)

Learning Objectives

There are five primary learning objectives for this course (and one bonus). I have written each of the objectives below, first in Sanskrit in anuṣṭubh meter, then in a rather literal 4-quarter English translation, and then a more vernacular prose explanation.

Objective 1: Creation and Cosmogonies

सद्धर्शनेऽभिषिक्ते च
सृष्टिं सम्यक् निरीप्यताम् ।
धर्मं ज्ञानं च बुध्यतां
पृथिवीं च विभावयेत् ॥

Within the saddarśana and abhiśikta traditions,
let creation be thoroughly examined;
let ethical and spiritual insights be discerned;
and may one also contemplate the Earth.

Explanation: I want you to explore the profound creation narratives across Hindu and Christian traditions, understand how they shape ethical and spiritual frameworks, and reflect on their implications for how we view and care for our planet.

Objective 2: Ludic Theology (लीला)

लीलां दिव्यां विमृश्यतां
तत्त्वे धर्मे च विद्यते ।
उत्साहाद्विधिना युक्ता
कल्पनां नूतनां नयेत् ॥

May divine play be contemplated,
which resides in both philosophy and ethics; through devoted ritual and enthusiasm,
let it lead to fresh creativity.

Explanation: I encourage you to ‘seriously’ contemplate the idea of divine play, recognize its theological depth, and allow this playful approach to inspire new ways of thinking and creating.

Objective 3: Interreligious Dialogue

नानादर्शेषु संवादः
पृथक् स्त्रीवाद लोकधिः ।
मोक्षं मार्गान् परामृश्य
सर्वेषां श्रेयसे क्रियत् ॥

Among diverse viewpoints, let there be dialogue;
consider feminist perspectives and worldly wisdom;
reflect upon paths of liberation;
and act for the welfare of all.

Explanation: I hope you'll engage meaningfully with different religious and philosophical viewpoints, incorporate diverse perspectives like feminist insights, and seek to contribute positively to a more just and compassionate world.

Objective 4: Samgraha-Vistara Method

संग्रहम् विस्तरं चापि
शास्त्रार्थे शिक्षणे सदा ।
विनियोगः सुविस्तीर्णो
आर्षे पथ्यांश्च बोधयेत् ॥

May one always practice both summary and expansion
in the study and teaching of sacred texts;
use broad applications wisely,
and illuminate the traditional pathways.

Explanation: I want you to understand the Indian theological method of condensing and expanding ideas (known as samgraha-vistara), as this will help you make sense of complex texts and apply their wisdom effectively in various contexts. (Also, though, this philosophy has deep and broad implications, imo.)

Objective 5: Synthesis of Core Themes

सृष्टिं सम्बन्धं ऐक्यं च
नानात्वं चेति संश्रयेत् ।
सभा-संलाप-पुस्तादिं
भावयेत् स्वान्तसिद्धये ॥

May one integrate creation, relationship, unity, and diversity as a shared foundation;
bringing them into discussions and writings,
thus cultivating inner realization.

Explanation: I aim for you to synthesize key themes like unity, diversity, and relationality into your discussions and reflections, allowing these insights to shape your personal growth and learning journey.

Bonus Objective 6: Karma

As a bonus objective, I aim for you to understand that the word karma means “action” or “work” or “Vedic ritual”; it is not any of the things that Taylor Swift says it is.

Attendance, Participation, and Quizzes (20%)

Come to class prepared to say something about the assignment. Writing down your question or comment in advance is certainly encouraged (but is not required). If you have done the reading assignment, then it should be easy to come up either with something to say about it or at least a good, specific question. If you are struggling to complete the readings efficiently, please come to my office hours or email me for an appointment.

We will occasionally have quizzes during class. These serve a wide variety of pedagogical functions, only some of which are evaluative. You will not be notified in advance of quizzes (besides this note) and graded quizzes will not be returned.

Your daily attendance, participation, and quiz grade is calculated based on a scale of 0 to 1:

  • If you arrive to class ≤ 10 min late, your max score for that day will be .9
  • If you arrive to class ≤ 10 min late and miss a quiz, max score for that day will be .8
  • If you arrive to class ≥ 10 min late, your max score for that day will be .8
  • If you violate Dr. Bannon’s electronic device policy, your participation grade for that class will be lowered commensurately
  • Sleeping in class or other inattentiveness incompatible with the endeavor to learn will be reflected in your participation score for that day.
  • Leaving the classroom during class for a reasonable period of time (~5 min) will have no affect on your participation score
  • If you need to leave class ≤ 15 min before the end of class time, simply inform Dr. Bannon about that in person immediately before that particular class and this will generally have little or no impact on your grade

Policy for Absences (Extra Credit)

Undergraduate students are adults. As such, I intend to treat you as adults. It is up to you to decide for yourself what is and what is not a valid reason to miss class. That is not for me to determine. Your reasons for missing class are your business, not mine. I treat all absences the same. If you miss class for any reason, it will obviously have an impact on your attendance/participation grade since you were not in attendance and did not participate. However, you may ‘make up’ any absence to receive full credit for that class. To do so, please follow these steps:

  1. Ensure that you are fully caught up with assignments due on the day of your absence. Take good notes on the assignment, as usual.
  2. Either listen to the audio recording or (when possible) watch the panopto video recording of class and take good notes, as usual.
  3. Either visit my campus office hour or schedule an online video meeting with me or attempt to connect with me on Discord.
  4. During the meeting, inform me which absence you are making up and be sure to have your notes ready. Simply tell me what you learned from the assignment, what you took away from class recording and ask any questions you may have. We’ll discuss the material together for about 10 minutes. So long as you are well-prepared for both, you may make-up two absences in a single meeting.

There is currently no limit to the number of times a student can take advantage of this policy. Generally speaking, make-up for absences should be completed within 3 weeks of the absence, unless there is a very good reason to extend this already generous policy. In most cases, I will permit students to complete this make-up assignment together with another student. Your grade for this make-up assignment will range from 0 to 1 based on how well you follow the instructions written above and this will be added directly to your participation grade.

NOTE: You may not complete the make-up assignment until you have met with Brad for the initial meeting, but you can schedule two meetings back-to-back to do both.

Leadership Days (10%)

Each student has been assigned a “leadership day” (A, B, C, or D). Click here to see your assignment. Each time that it is your leadership day, this simply means that you should come to class slightly more prepared than other days. You should make extra effort to lead small group discussions and extra effort to participate in large group discussions. Dr. Bannon is more likely to cold call on leaders during class.

The default grade for your leadership days grade is 90%. This grade will increase/decrease based on the quality of your leadership on your leadership days.

You may NOT lead podcast discussion on the same text that you discussed on your leadership day. For example, if you are in Leadership Group “A”, then you should come to class on March 18 prepared to lead a small portion of the class discussion about a passage from the Gita reading AND you should come to your podcast 4 team meeting prepared to lead your podcast team on a passage from John’s Gospel.

Why do this? Leadership days perform a variety of pedagogical functions. Some students are very comfortable speaking in class; many are not. If you are very comfortable speaking in class, it would be best to take a small step back on days that are not your leadership day. Students who are generally reluctant to speak in class often find this system helpful.

There is another significant reason that I use this system specifically for this comparative theology class. A core learning objective of this course is for students to become familiar with both the Hindu and Christian scriptures and theologies. The leadership day structure efficiently ensures that each student focuses appropriate intellectual energy towards each of these two traditions, sometimes in class and sometimes in the podcast discussions.

What if I am absent on a leadership day? If it is necessary for you to be absent on one of your assigned leadership days, you have two options: (1) Don’t sweat it. Each student can miss one of their leadership days without consequence (but it will still affect your attendance grade, of course). (2) Swap with someone else. However, group “A” leaders may only swap with group “C” (and vice-versa) and group “B” leaders may only swap with group “D” leaders (and vice versa). As long as the two of you agree, you can simply inform Dr. Bannon of the swap.

Podcast Assignment (50%):

Discourse is a centrally important part of theology (in particular) and the humanities (in general). Discussion is also profoundly important for good citizenship and the functioning of a democratic society. Having sincere, meaningful discussions with people who are different from you is not easy, particularly in relation to topics about religion, theology, and ethics. Fortunately, discourse is a skill which can be practiced and honed. The “podcast” assignment is designed to do just that.

Near the beginning of the semester, students will be placed into small teams of (usually) 4 or 5. I attempt to diversify the teams as much as possible, especially in terms of gender, year (freshman, senior, etc), and college major.

In general, podcast meetings should last 20-25 minutes long (about 5x minutes where x=number of people on your team that are present). Please feel free to meet in person OR by video conference using something like Zoom or Google Meet, etc.

Record the meeting. If you meet in person, audio-only recordings are perfectly fine, but please try to use one another’s names as often as possible so that I know who is speaking. If you meet by Zoom or Google Meet, etc., then please record the video and audio. Have one person share the recording with me by saving it in your team’s shared folder, which I will create for each team. ONLY I and people in your group have permission/access to your team folder. Your recordings will be kept entirely private and will not be shared by me and should not be shared by you unless you have express, written consent from every single person on your team.

For some podcast episodes, I will give you some discussion prompts – but please feel free to deviate from these suggestions. If there is something in the course material that you want to discuss with the group, then please bring it up! 

Podcast Format

The format of the podcasts is very important: Each and every student on the team should take a turn leading the discussion in each and every podcast. All students are expected to share the leadership role equally. For example, in a 20 minute podcast with 4 people, each person on the team should lead the discussion for about 5 minutes (NOT monologue for 5 min!). The BEST way to do this is by:

(1) Select a SPECIFIC passage from a reading assignment AND/OR a specific topic discussed in class. IMPORTANT: The passage you choose MUST NOT be from the same religious tradition as your leadership day in that unit. For example, if you are a group “A” leader and led discussion about a Christian text in class during that unit, then you must focus on a Hindu text in your podcast for that unit.

(2) Explain why you chose this particular passage.

(3) Start a discussion about the passage in which everyone on your team will participate.

Your performance in this assignment will be evaluated not only on how well you have engaged the course content, but also how well you listen and respond to other students on your team. A key objective of this assignment is to enable students to practice and model the phenomenological method of discourse. ALL GRADES ARE INDIVIDUAL.

The podcast “episodes” are not equally weighted. For the first few podcasts, students generally receive full credit by simply following the instructions above and participating in each recorded discussion. The middle podcasts are worth slightly more and are also graded on the quality of your contribution to the discussion. The final podcast is weighted most heavily. Having good, meaningful discussions takes practice and comfortability with your podcast teammates. I expect to see improvement and growth of understanding as you gain experience. If you follow these instructions, listen carefully to my feedback, and make adjustments accordingly, then you will not only earn a good grade but will also take much away from each discussion with your peers. In most cases, podcast mates bond closely with one another and find the overall experience to be both enlightening and enjoyable.

Artificial Intelligence Policy

Artificial Intelligence has become widely available and is becoming increasingly powerful. While this technology is not especially new, its capability and availability are rapidly evolving. Like any powerful tool, AI is having an enormous impact on education, theology, and ethics, both positive and negative. In this course, I will make use of this new technology, often in the form of experimentation and exploration, and students will be actively encouraged to make use of this new and powerful tool, as well.

However, Dr. Bannon emphasizes that this new technology is unpredictable: It can hallucinate, it can provide false information, and it is deeply encoded with cultural, historical, religious, and other biases that sometimes (but not always) reflect its training models and frequently mirrors the biases and assumptions of users. In part, our engagement with and use of these technologies is motivated by a need to dis/cover these hidden biases. Dr. Bannon emphasizes that they are not responsible for any content generated by these tools and encourages all students to think critically and skeptically about any AI generated content.

That said, Dr. Bannon makes frequent use of AI tools and strongly encourages students to learn to use these tools so as to be competent to enter the workplace after graduation. These tools are especially powerful and useful as tools to parse, lemmatize, analyze, translate, and understand language, including sacred texts. Hence, students who are able to competently deploy these powerful tools to better analyze and interpret sacred texts in Sanskrit, Hebrew, and Greek are likely to cultivate deeper understanding of these ancient repositories of human wisdom in contrast to students lacking competency in these rapidly advancing artefacts.

As these technological tools advance, they become increasingly pedagogically valuable and potent. Hence, Dr. Bannon continues to explore and innovate new ways to harness and deploy these technes to teach more efficiently and effectively. I do so mindful of potential downsides, privacy concerns, and FERPA. I make every attempt to use AI tools responsibly, mindfully, and fairly.

If any student has any questions about how I use AI, I am more than happy to devote time and effort outside of class (in office hours, online meetings, or Discord hours, etc.) towards that end.

Commitment to Inclusion

This course is a combined lecture and discussion course. This means that, in part, all students are responsible for contributing to both their own learning experience and the learning experience of others. Because the contribution of ideas from each student is critical to the learning process, any behavior that makes other students feel uncomfortable in their learning environment will not be tolerated. This includes interrupting others while they are talking, carrying on conversations separate from the class discussion, or making comments that could be perceived as offensive in terms of race, gender, sexual orientation, religion, ethnicity, nationality, social-economic status, ability, etc. Please make every effort to maintain an atmosphere where everyone feels comfortable sharing and responding to ideas. (Resources for Gender-Inclusive Language in the Classroom 3 CITL Presentation, March 15, 2016)
