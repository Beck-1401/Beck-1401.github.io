Class Report: Genesis 1-3 and Ancient Near Eastern Context

Synopsis

This class session focused on the first three chapters of Genesis. Brad began by providing background on the Hebrew text, emphasizing its origins in oral tradition, the importance of memorization, and the historical context (likely the Maccabean revolt and Roman occupation) that led to its being written down by communities like the Essenes (associated with the Dead Sea Scrolls). They discussed the different names used for God in the text, particularly the distinction between Elohim (used predominantly in Genesis 1) and the Tetragrammaton (YHWH, often substituted with Hashem or Adonai, appearing more in Genesis 2-3), and how these reflect different traditions. The class then engaged in a discussion of Genesis 1, noting its poetic structure, themes of creation's goodness ("and God saw that it was good"), the use of contrast and bifurcation, the plural "us" in the creation of humankind ("in our image"), the question of evil's origin, and the initial mandate for humans (be fruitful, multiply, master/tend the earth, with plants specified as food). Following this, the discussion shifted to Genesis 2 and 3, comparing and contrasting them with Genesis 1. Key differences highlighted included the more "hands-on" creation method in Genesis 2 (forming from dust) versus creation by speech in Genesis 1, the order of creation, the specific location details (Garden of Eden, Tigris, Euphrates), the creation of woman from man, and the introduction of the forbidden Tree of Knowledge. The role of the woman and potential interpretations were briefly touched upon. Finally, Brad introduced the Babylonian creation myth, the Enuma Elish (featuring Tiamat and Marduk), as crucial context. They explained how Genesis 1, particularly its assertion that humans are made in God's image and likeness (not as slaves, unlike in the Enuma Elish) and the institution of the Sabbath rest, can be read as a liberative counter-narrative developed during the Hebrews' exile or enslavement. The session concluded with a brief mention of the next reading assignment from the Ṛgveda.

Vocabulary

  • Genesis - The first book of the Hebrew Bible/Old Testament, discussed extensively.
  • Hebrew - The original language of the Genesis text.
  • BCE - Before Common Era, used for dating the text's solidification (around 1000 BCE).
  • Vedas - Ancient Hindu scriptures mentioned for comparison regarding oral tradition and memorization.
  • Tanakh (תַּנַ״ךְ) - The Hebrew Bible.
  • Essenes - A Jewish sect, likely responsible for writing/preserving the Dead Sea Scrolls.
  • Dead Sea Scrolls - Ancient manuscripts found near the Dead Sea, containing parts of the Hebrew Bible.
  • Tetragrammaton (יהוה) - The four-letter Hebrew name for God (YHWH), considered unpronounceable in Jewish tradition. Appears in Genesis 2-3.
  • Hashem (הַשֵּׁם) - Hebrew term meaning "The Name," used by observant Jews as a substitute for pronouncing the Tetragrammaton. (Composed of *ha* - the, and *shem* - name).
  • Adonai (אֲדֹנָי) - Hebrew term meaning "My Lord," also used as a substitute for the Tetragrammaton. Often translated as "the Lord."
  • Elohim (אֱלֹהִים) - A Hebrew name for God, grammatically plural but often used with singular verbs. The primary name for God in Genesis 1. Derived from *El* (god, lowercase g).
  • Hashamayim (הַשָּׁמַיִם) - Hebrew for "the heavens," noted for its plural ending (-im) similar to Elohim.
  • Leviathan (לִוְיָתָן) - A great sea monster mentioned in reference to the Book of Job and creation.
  • Enuma Elish - The Babylonian creation myth, discussed as context for Genesis.
  • Tiamat - The primordial goddess (mother) in the Enuma Elish, killed by Marduk, her body becoming the earth.
  • Marduk - A chief god in Babylonian mythology who kills Tiamat and creates humans from his father's blood and earth to be slaves.
  • Ṛgveda (ऋग्वेद) - The oldest of the Hindu Vedas, mentioned as the reading for the next class.

Key Takeaways

  • The Hebrew Bible, including Genesis, has deep roots in oral tradition, where memorization preceded interpretation.
  • The transition from oral to written text was likely spurred by existential threats to the Jewish community, leading to efforts like those of the Essenes.
  • Different names for God in Genesis (Elohim vs. YHWH/Tetragrammaton) likely reflect distinct historical traditions woven together.
  • Genesis 1 presents creation as inherently "good," occurring through divine speech, and emphasizes order, separation (contrast), and balance.
  • The phrase "let us make humankind in our image" (Genesis 1:26) raises questions about plurality and the divine nature.
  • Genesis 1 initially designates plants as food for humans and animals.
  • Genesis 2-3 offers a different creation narrative: more anthropomorphic (God forming, planting), focused on a specific location (Eden), detailing the creation of woman from man, and introducing the prohibition of the Tree of Knowledge.
  • Understanding the contemporary Babylonian myth, Enuma Elish (where humans are created as slaves to the gods), highlights the potentially liberative message of Genesis 1, which posits humans are created in God's image and likeness, not as slaves, and are granted Sabbath rest.
  • Interpreting biblical texts requires attention to historical context, linguistic details, and literary structure.
