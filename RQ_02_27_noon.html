College Class Report: Hinduism and Philosophy Review Game

Synopsis

This class session began with a brief meditation led by Brad. The main activity was a team-based review game, formatted similarly to Jeopardy, covering concepts from Hinduism and related philosophical ideas. Brad explained the rules: teams would choose categories, answer questions within a time limit, and could steal points if the initial team failed. Topics included Greek philosophical terms, details about Krishna (his mother Yashoda, the Rāslīlā dance, his love for butter, subduing Kaliya), Mahāvākyas (Great Sayings) from the Upaniṣads, the Ṣaḍ Darśanas (six orthodox schools of Hindu philosophy like Nyāya, Vaiśeṣika, Mīmāṃsā), key figures and texts (Durgā, Sītā, Rāvaṇa, Rāmāyaṇam, Purāṇas, Upaniṣads), and concepts like Brahman and Jñāna Mārga. Team 13 emerged as the winner. As a fun conclusion, Brad performed some TikTok/Fortnite dances and a short rap for the class. The session ended with logistical discussions regarding office hours and a podcast assignment due after spring break.

Key Takeaways

  • The class reviewed core concepts of Hinduism through an interactive game format.
  • Understanding the Ṣaḍ Darśanas (six orthodox schools) like Nyāya (logic), Vaiśeṣika (atomism), and Mīmāṃsā (ritual interpretation) is important.
  • Mahāvākyas (Great Sayings) like "Aham Brahmāsmi" (I am Brahman) and "Ayam Ātmā Brahma" (This Self is Brahman) encapsulate key Upaniṣadic teachings.
  • Distinctions between theological approaches are significant: Apophatic (via negativa, Nirguṇa Brahman - describing God by negation) vs. Cataphatic (via positiva, Saguṇa Brahman - describing God with positive attributes).
  • Līlā (divine play) is a key concept for understanding deities like Krishna, illustrated by stories like the Rāslīlā, stealing butter (Makhan Chor), and dancing on the serpent Kāliya.
  • Knowledge of key figures (Krishna, Yashoda, Durgā, Sītā, Rāvaṇa) and foundational texts (Upaniṣads, Rāmāyaṇam, Purāṇas, Brāhmaṇas) is crucial.
  • Jñāna Mārga (Path of Knowledge) is one of the primary paths to salvation, involving study and understanding reality.
  • Soteriology is the academic term for the study of salvation.
  • The concept of Brahman represents the ultimate reality, which can be conceived with (Saguṇa) or without (Nirguṇa) attributes.
