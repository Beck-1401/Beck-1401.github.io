Class Discussion: Gospel of John, Chapters 1-5

Synopsis

This class session focused on an in-depth discussion of the first five chapters of the Gospel of John. Brad opened the class, mentioning their seasonal allergies, and then guided the students into analyzing the text. The discussion began with the Prologue (John 1:1-18), exploring themes of knowing versus not knowing, direct perception (linking to *pramāṇa*), the nature of the Word (*logos*), light and darkness, and the role of John the Baptist as a witness. Students drew parallels between the creation narrative in Genesis 1/Proverbs 8 and John's opening verses. The identity and titles of Jesus were a major focus, including "Lamb of God," "Son of God," "Son of Man," and "Messiah," with discussions on their significance and potential interpretations (e.g., sacrifice, humility, divinity, humanity). The concept of Jesus's miracles as "signs" (*sēmeia*) pointing to deeper truths was highlighted, particularly the water-to-wine miracle and the healing in chapter 5 (the "sign that failed"). The interaction with Nicodemus brought up the theme of being "born again" or "born from above" through water and Spirit. The encounter with the Samaritan woman illustrated Jesus crossing social boundaries and offering "living water." The class also touched upon the relationship between Father, Son, and Spirit, the nature of the *logos* in Greek thought, the concept of *kenosis*, the body as a temple, and the notable absence of Jesus's baptism scene in John's account. Throughout the discussion, Brad encouraged students to focus on the text itself and consider its unique theological perspective.

Vocabulary

*imago dei* (Latin)
Image of God. Discussed in relation to John the Baptist potentially being a physical representation allowing humans to grasp divine concepts (related to Genesis 1:26-27).
*kenosis* (Greek: κένωσις)
Emptying, self-emptying. Mentioned in relation to Jesus being divine yet in human form, letting the divine reside within, and potentially as a prerequisite for receiving the Spirit.
*līlā* (Sanskrit: लीला)
Divine play or sport. A student connected God sending Jesus to the concept of *līlā* as God's play, though acknowledging it might be a stretch.
*logos* (Greek: λόγος)
Word, reason, logic, divine principle. Discussed extensively regarding John 1:1 ("In the beginning was the Word..."). Brad noted its deep significance in Greek philosophy and theology, beyond just "word," encompassing logic and foundational principles. Its divine nature in the context of John 1:1 was explored.
*pramāṇa* (Sanskrit: प्रमाण)
Valid means or source of knowledge. A student connected the theme of knowing/not knowing in John 1 to the concept of *pramāṇa*, particularly the idea that direct perception (a *pramāṇa*) was lacking for many regarding Jesus initially.
*pros* (Greek: πρός)
Towards, with, near. Discussed during the grammatical analysis of John 1:1 ("the Word was *pros* God"), suggesting an orientation or directionality towards God.
*Rūaḥ ʾĔlōhīm* (Hebrew: רוּחַ אֱלֹהִים)
Spirit/Wind/Breath of God. Mentioned by Brad in connection with Genesis 1:2 and the discussion of the Spirit/wind (*pneuma*) in John 3:8, highlighting the shared meaning of wind, breath, and spirit.
*theos* (Greek: θεός)
God, divine. Discussed in the detailed analysis of John 1:1, where Brad pointed out the different forms (*theon*, *theos*) and the lack of the definite article before *theos* in the phrase "καὶ θεὸς ἦν ὁ λόγος," suggesting a possible translation like "divine was the Word" instead of "the Word was God."

Key Takeaways

  • John's Gospel heavily emphasizes themes of knowing/not knowing, seeing/believing, and direct perception as a source of knowledge (or lack thereof).
  • The Prologue (John 1:1-18) establishes foundational concepts: the divine *Logos* (Word) as the agent of creation, the source of life and light, existing eternally with God and being divine itself. This echoes Genesis 1 and Proverbs 8.
  • Jesus is presented through multiple significant titles (Lamb of God, Son of God, Son of Man, Messiah, Rabbi), each contributing to a complex portrait of his identity and mission.
  • Miracles in John are explicitly called "signs" (*sēmeia*), indicating they are performed with the specific purpose of revealing Jesus' identity and glory, rather than being mere acts of power.
  • The relationship between witnessing signs and achieving faith is complex; seeing does not always equate to believing, as shown in the rejection following the healing in Chapter 5.
  • The concept of being "born again" or "born from above" (John 3) signifies a necessary spiritual transformation through water and Spirit to perceive and enter the Kingdom of God.
  • The incarnation ("the Word became flesh") is central, emphasizing Jesus's dual nature as both fully divine and fully human.
  • John the Baptist serves as a crucial prophetic witness, preparing the way and identifying Jesus, but explicitly stating he is not the Light himself.
  • John's Gospel uses rich symbolism (light/darkness, water, wind/spirit) and dialogues (e.g., with Nicodemus, the Samaritan woman) to convey theological truths.
  • Unlike Matthew and Luke, John provides no birth narrative; Jesus's public significance begins with his identification by John the Baptist and the descent of the Spirit, potentially marking his "second birth" as the Son of God in this context.
  • The Gospel may reflect the context of a later community facing exclusion, emphasizing Jesus's divinity and the importance of belief in him for eternal life and belonging.
