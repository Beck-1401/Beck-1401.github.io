Vocabulary

  • līlā (लीला): Divine play, cosmic play; discussed extensively regarding performance, ritual, Kṛṣṇa's actions, and as the central theme for the upcoming podcast.
  • nirguṇa (निर्गुण): Without qualities; often used to describe the ultimate reality (*Brahman*) in *Advaita Vedānta*.
  • saguṇa (सगुण): With qualities; often used to describe God or *Brahman* as possessing attributes in theistic traditions like *Viśiṣṭādvaita*.
  • cataphatic theology: Theological approach describing God through positive affirmations (what God *is*).
  • apophatic theology: Theological approach describing God through negation (what God *is not*).
  • Kṛṣṇa (कृष्ण): An important deity in Hinduism, considered an avatar of Viṣṇu; central figure in the Johnston Lørgen readings and the Bhagavad Gītā.
  • Yaśodā (यशोदा): Kṛṣṇa's adoptive mother, featured in the story where she sees the universe in his mouth.
  • Rāsa Līlā (रास लीला): Kṛṣṇa's divine circular dance with the cowherds (*gopīs*), an example of *līlā*.
  • soteriology: The study of salvation or liberation. Discussed in relation to Johnston Lørgen Chapter 3 and how Kṛṣṇa saves.
  • mokṣa (मोक्ष): Liberation or release from the cycle of birth and death (*saṃsāra*).
  • Advaita Vedānta (अद्वैत वेदान्त): Non-dualistic school of Hindu philosophy emphasizing the ultimate identity of the individual self (*Ātman*) and the ultimate reality (*Brahman*). A topic for podcast review.
  • mahāvākyas (महावाक्य): The "great sayings" from the Upaniṣads, encapsulating key Vedāntic truths (e.g., "Tat tvam asi" - That thou art). Mentioned as potentially relevant for the *Advaita* review.
  • Brahman (ब्रह्मन्): The ultimate, unchanging reality in Hindu philosophy.
  • Viśiṣṭādvaita (विशिष्टाद्वैत): Qualified non-dualism; a school of Vedānta (associated with Rāmānuja) that affirms *Brahman* as the ultimate reality but one which possesses distinct attributes, including the world and individual souls. Contrasted with *Advaita*.
  • Bhagavad Gītā (भगवद् गीता): "Song of the Lord"; a central Hindu scripture detailing a conversation between Kṛṣṇa and Arjuna. To be studied in depth after the break.
  • dharma (धर्म): Duty, righteousness, law, intrinsic nature. Mentioned as a term often translated by Barbara Stoler Miller.
  • yoga (योग): Union, discipline, path. Mentioned in relation to Graham Schweig's translation and its focus.
  • Ātman (आत्मन्): The individual self or soul.
  • pāda (पाद): Foot, quarter, section (referring to poetic structure in the Gītā).
  • Devanāgarī (देवनागरी): The script used for writing Sanskrit and other North Indian languages. Mentioned as included in Schweig's Gītā translation.
  • pramāṇa (प्रमाण): A valid means or source of knowledge in Indian philosophies.
  • Ṣaḍdarśana (षड्दर्शन): The six orthodox schools (*darśanas*) of Hindu philosophy.
  • darśana (दर्शन): Perspective, viewpoint, philosophical school.
  • pratyakṣa (प्रत्यक्ष): Direct perception through the senses; considered the most reliable *pramāṇa*. (From *prati* 'before' + *akṣa* 'eyes').
  • akṣa (अक्ष): Eye.
  • Mīmāṃsā (मीमांसा): One of the six orthodox schools, focused on Vedic interpretation and ritual duty. (*Mīmāṃsakas* are adherents).
  • anumāna (अनुमान): Logical inference; knowledge derived from prior perception (e.g., inferring fire from smoke). (From *anu* 'after' + *māna* 'thinking').
  • anu (अनु): After.
  • māna (मान): Thinking, measuring.
  • śabda (शब्द): Word, verbal testimony; specifically, the authority of the Vedas as a source of knowledge, particularly regarding duty (*dharma*).
  • abhāva (अभाव): Non-existence, absence; considered a distinct *pramāṇa* by some schools (like Kumārila Bhaṭṭa's branch of Mīmāṃsā), revealing knowledge through absence. (From *a* 'not' + *bhāva* 'being').
  • bhāva (भाव): Being, existence, state.
  • Kumārila Bhaṭṭa (कुमारिल भट्ट): An influential 7th-century Mīmāṃsā philosopher known for his defense of Vedic authority and his discussion of *pramāṇas*, including *abhāva*.
  • Vedānta (वेदान्त): "End of the Vedas"; philosophical traditions based on the Upaniṣads. Sister school to Mīmāṃsā.
  • satkāryavāda (सत्कार्यवाद): The theory of causality asserting that the effect pre-exists within its material cause. Mentioned briefly in relation to Vedānta.

Key Takeaways

  • The concept of *līlā* (divine play) is crucial for understanding Kṛṣṇa's actions, certain Hindu rituals/performances, and has broader philosophical implications. It will be the central focus of the next podcast.
  • The upcoming podcast requires synthesizing information from William Sax on *līlā*, Johnston Lørgen on Kṛṣṇa's life and soteriology (how he saves/liberates), and a review of *Advaita Vedānta* principles.
  • Understanding the distinction between *Advaita Vedānta* (non-dual, non-theistic) and *Viśiṣṭādvaita* (qualified non-dual, theistic) is essential for interpreting Hindu texts like the Bhagavad Gītā from different philosophical viewpoints.
  • *Pramāṇas* are the valid means of acquiring knowledge in Hindu philosophy. Key ones include *pratyakṣa* (perception), *anumāna* (inference), and *śabda* (Vedic testimony).
  • *Abhāva* (non-existence) is presented by thinkers like Kumārila Bhaṭṭa as a unique *pramāṇa* that reveals the nature of causality: a cause only truly becomes the cause of its effect at the precise moment the cause ceases to exist in its original form (e.g., milk becoming curd).
  • This understanding of causality via *abhāva* has significant implications, particularly when considering concepts like a creator and creation – suggesting the creator must cease to be the creator to become the creation.
  • Class logistics: A review game is planned for Thursday, the podcast deadline is flexible around Spring Break, and a no-screen policy (laptops/tablets) will be implemented after the break, requiring printed or physical texts.
