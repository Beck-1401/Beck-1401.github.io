Class Report: Bhagavad Gītā - Chapters 1 & 2 Discussion

Synopsis

This class session began with a brief meditation, followed by an engaging Jenga game played by the designated discussion leaders, serving as a practical example of *līlā* (play). After confirming attendance, the class delved into the first two chapters of the *Bhagavad Gītā*. The discussion explored Arjuna's profound internal conflict in Chapter 1 regarding fighting his kinsmen and teachers, weighing familial duty against his role as a warrior. Chapter 2 formed the core of the discussion, focusing on Krishna's counsel. Key themes included the nature of duty (*dharma*), the importance of action (*karma*) without attachment to its fruits, the concept of the eternal and indestructible self (*ātman*), the role of discipline and understanding, the different paths to liberation (*Karma Mārga*, *Jñāna Mārga*, *Bhakti Mārga*), and the need for emotional detachment and objectivity in fulfilling one's role. Students actively engaged, questioning the source of duty, the application of these teachings beyond war, and the relationship between the individual self and the ultimate reality (Brahman).

Vocabulary

  • Bhagavad Gītā (भगवद् गीता): The sacred Hindu scripture being discussed, part of the Mahābhārata epic.
  • Līlā (लीला): Sanskrit term for 'play', 'sport', or 'pastime', often used to describe divine action or the cosmic play of creation. Mentioned in the context of the Jenga game.
  • Dharma (धर्म): A core concept in Hinduism meaning duty, righteousness, cosmic law, ethics, or one's essential nature/role in life. Central to Arjuna's conflict and Krishna's teachings.
  • Advaita Vedānta (अद्वैत वेदान्त): A school of Hindu philosophy emphasizing non-dualism, the identity of the individual self (Ātman) and the ultimate reality (Brahman). Mentioned as a perspective for interpreting duty.
  • Viśiṣṭādvaita Vedānta (विशिष्टाद्वैत वेदान्त): A school of Hindu philosophy emphasizing qualified non-dualism, where the individual self and the world are real but dependent on God (Brahman). Mentioned as an alternative perspective on duty.
  • Caste: The social stratification system in traditional Hindu society. Arjuna expresses concern about the intermixture of castes resulting from the war.
  • Viṣṇu (विष्णु): One of the principal deities of Hinduism, the preserver and sustainer of the universe. Krishna is considered an avatar (incarnation) of Viṣṇu.
  • Brahmā (ब्रह्मा): The Hindu god of creation.
  • Śiva (शिव): The Hindu god of destruction and transformation.
  • Ātman (आत्मन्): Sanskrit term for the individual self or soul, considered eternal and indestructible in the Gītā. Discussed in relation to death and killing.
  • Brahman (ब्रह्मन्): The ultimate, unchanging reality in Hinduism, the supreme cosmic spirit. Discussed in relation to the manifestation of individual selves.
  • Karma Mārga (कर्म मार्ग): The path of action; one of the three primary paths to liberation mentioned by Brad, emphasizing selfless duty and ritual.
  • Jñāna Mārga (ज्ञान मार्ग): The path of knowledge; one of the three primary paths, emphasizing wisdom and understanding the nature of reality (often associated with Advaita Vedānta).
  • Bhakti Mārga (भक्ति मार्ग): The path of devotion; one of the three primary paths, emphasizing love and surrender to God.
  • Karma (कर्म): Action, work, or deed; also refers to the principle of cause and effect. Discussed in the context of performing actions and their traces (saṃskāra).
  • Saṃskāra (संस्कार): Mental impressions, recollections, or psychological imprints left by actions (karma), shaping habits and tendencies. Explained by Brad using the cigarette analogy.

Key Takeaways

  • Arjuna's crisis in Chapter 1 exemplifies the complex conflict between personal relationships/ethics and societal/caste duty (*dharma*).
  • Krishna's core advice centers on performing one's prescribed duty (*dharma*) diligently but without attachment to the results or fruits of the action.
  • The concept of the eternal, unchanging Self (*Ātman*) is central to Krishna's argument, reframing death as a transition rather than an end, thus justifying Arjuna's duty to fight.
  • Discipline is essential – discipline in action (performing duties), discipline in understanding (seeking knowledge/wisdom), and discipline in devotion (focusing on the divine).
  • The *Bhagavad Gītā* synthesizes multiple valid paths (*mārgas*) towards spiritual goals: selfless action (*Karma*), knowledge (*Jñāna*), and devotion (*Bhakti*).
  • Achieving inner peace requires detachment from desires, possessiveness, ego, attraction, and aversion, allowing for objective action.
  • While set in the context of war, the principles discussed (duty, detachment, self-control, understanding the nature of self) have broad applications to everyday life and ethical decision-making.
  • Concepts like heaven and hell, within this context, are often viewed as temporary states resulting from actions, not eternal destinations, with the cycle of rebirth continuing until liberation.
