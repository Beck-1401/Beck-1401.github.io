THEO 1432: The Religious Quest
Comprehensive Study Guide

Introduction

Welcome to the study guide for THEO 1432: The Religious Quest. This guide synthesizes lectures, discussions, readings, and key concepts covered throughout the semester, focusing on the comparative study of Hindu and Christian sacred texts. Use the navigation bar above or the Table of Contents (on wider screens) to jump between sections. The goal is to provide a helpful resource for reviewing material, preparing for assignments (like podcasts and the midterm), and consolidating your understanding of the course's core themes.

Remember Brad's emphasis: theology is faith seeking understanding (*fides quaerens intellectum*), a dynamic process anchored in sacred texts. Engage with this material actively, questioning and seeking connections.

Overall Course Themes

Several recurring themes connect the diverse texts and traditions studied:

  • Creation & Causality: Exploring narratives of origin (Genesis, Vedic hymns, Purāṇas) and philosophical concepts of material, efficient, and final causes.
  • Nature of Reality: Examining concepts like non-dualism (Advaita), qualified non-dualism (Viśiṣṭādvaita), the relationship between the One and the Many, and the interplay of matter and spirit/energy.
  • Divine Play (Līlā): Understanding creation and divine action not just as purposeful but also as spontaneous, joyful play.
  • Ethics & Duty (Dharma): Investigating moral responsibility, right action, and one's role within cosmic and social order.
  • Knowledge & Ignorance: Exploring paths to wisdom (Jñāna Mārga), the nature of consciousness, and the overcoming of ignorance (avidyā).
  • Devotion (Bhakti): Understanding the path of love and devotion to a personal deity (Kṛṣṇa, Devī, Christ).
  • Scriptural Interpretation (Hermeneutics): Recognizing the importance of context, language, genre (Śruti, Smṛti, Purāṇa, Gospel), and interpretive methods (allegory, Mīmāṃsā rules, Saṅgraha-Vistara).
  • Self & Identity: Questioning the nature of the self (Ātman, "I"), its relationship to the divine (Brahman, God), and its role in the world.
  • Comparative Method: Engaging deeply with individual traditions before allowing comparisons to emerge organically.

Unit Summaries

Foundations & Methods (Approx. Jan 14 - Jan 21)

The course began by establishing its theological framework, distinguishing it from religious studies. Key methods like the Saṅgraha-Vistara approach (summary/elaboration) were introduced. Foundational concepts like the Vedic canon (Śruti, Vedas structure), the Ṣaḍ-darśana (six orthodox schools), and particularly the Mīmāṃsā school (ritual focus, hermeneutics, arthavāda, dharma as "what is to be done") were covered.

Brad emphasized: "Theology is philosophical discourse anchored in canonical texts."

Genesis 1-3 & Creation (Approx. Jan 16 - Jan 30)

Focus on the Hebrew creation accounts. Discussion covered oral tradition vs. written text (Essenes), names of God (Elohim vs. YHWH/Tetragrammaton), the goodness of creation, creation by speech vs. hands-on forming, the concept of Imago Dei, human purpose (dominion/tending), the Sabbath, the Tree of Knowledge, and the contrast between Genesis 1 and 2-3. Crucial context was provided by comparing Genesis 1 to the Babylonian *Enūma Eliš*, highlighting Genesis as a potentially liberative counter-narrative against the idea of humans as slaves to gods. Rashi's interpretation of *bara* (separate) and *creatio continua* was introduced.

Key insight: Reading Genesis 1 against the *Enūma Eliš* reveals its message that humans are made in God's image, not as slaves.

Vedic Hymns (ṚgVeda) (Approx. Jan 21)

Exploration of three ancient hymns: Nāsadīya Sūkta (X.129), Hiraṇyagarbha Sūkta (X.121), and Puruṣa Sūkta (X.90). Themes included radical questioning of origins beyond being/non-being (Nāsadīya), creation from a "Golden Embryo" (Hiraṇyagarbha), and creation via the cosmic sacrifice of the primordial Person (Puruṣa). Read through the lens of Mīmāṃsā, these hymns function as *arthavāda* (motivating ritual action) rather than purely historical or descriptive accounts.

Proverbs 8-9 & Wisdom (Approx. Jan 23)

Analysis of Wisdom personified as a feminine figure (Ḥokmāh/Sophia). Discussion centered on the contrast between Wisdom in Ch. 8 and the "stupid woman" in Ch. 9, the tension with Genesis 3's view of seeking knowledge, Wisdom's pre-existence before creation (acting as a "prequel" to Genesis 1), and the meaning of "fear of the Lord." Connections were drawn to Genesis 1 (creation via Word/Logos) and the significance of the Greek *Sophia* for early Christians.

Devī Māhātmyam (Approx. Jan 28)

Introduction to Devī worship (Śāktism). Distinction between Śruti and Smṛti scriptures, placing the Devī Māhātmyam (part of Mārkaṇḍeya Purāṇa) in the Smṛti category. The text was interpreted allegorically: the frame story (Suratha, Samādhi, Medhas representing body, spirit, mind) and the battles against demons (Mahīṣāsura, Raktabīja) symbolizing inner struggles against ignorance (Tamas), passion/ego (Rajas), leading to purity (Sattva). The nature of Śakti (divine power) and the iconography of Durgā and Kālī were discussed.

Advaita Vedānta & Mahāvākyas (Approx. Feb 4 - Feb 13)

Deep dive into Advaita Vedānta (non-dualism). Review of Vedānta vs. Mīmāṃsā. Introduction of the four Mahāvākyas ("Great Sayings"). Focused study of the *Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad* (Ahaṃ Brahmāsmi - "I am Brahman") and the *Chāndogya Upaniṣad* (Tat Tvam Asi - "Thou Art That"). Analogies (clay, rivers, honey, salt, seeds) explored the relationship between the particular self (Ātman) and the universal reality (Brahman). Concepts of creation as unfolding (*śṛṣṭi*), the meaning of "I" (Aham), causality, and the Trimārga were discussed. Introduction to the *Aitareya Upaniṣad* (Prajñānam Brahma - "Consciousness is Brahman") and *Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad* (Ayam Ātmā Brahma - "This Self is Brahman"), emphasizing interpretive harmony (*Samanvaya*). Analysis of Om and the four states of consciousness (waking, dream, deep sleep, Turīya).

Brad's key analogy: The coin - ontologically one, but with ontic distinctions (heads/tails, knower/known).

Līlā & Kṛṣṇa (Approx. Feb 20 - Feb 25)

Shift towards Viśiṣṭādvaita (qualified non-dualism, theistic) and the concept of Līlā (divine play). Review of Vedānta schools, causality (material, efficient, final/Līlā), and introduction of *Abhāva Pramāṇa* (knowledge via absence, using milk-to-curd example from Kumārila Bhaṭṭa) and *Satkāryavāda* (effect pre-exists in cause). Introduction to Kṛṣṇa via the Bhāgavata Purāṇa, discussion of *avatāra* concept (descent), and stories illustrating Līlā (Kṛṣṇa eating mud, dancing on Kaliya). Readings from Sachs and Johnston Lørgen explored *līlā*'s meanings and Kṛṣṇa's *soteriology* (how he saves).

Bhagavad Gītā (Approx. Mar 11 - Mar 25)

Introduction to the Bhagavad Gītā: context within the Mahābhārata (Pāṇḍavas vs. Kauravas), Arjuna's crisis, Kṛṣṇa's role as charioteer/guide. Discussion of Chapters 1-5. Key themes: *dharma* (duty), *karma* (action, reinterpreted beyond ritual), detachment from fruits (*niṣkāma karma*), the eternal self (*Ātman*) vs. the perishable body, sacrifice (*yajña*, reinterpreted), desire as an obstacle, *lokasaṅgraha* (world-maintenance), and integrating the three *mārgas* (Karma, Jñāna, Bhakti). Readings by Stoler Miller emphasized. Comparisons drawn to Gandhi's interpretation and the Kaṭha Upaniṣad's chariot allegory.

Gospel of John (Approx. Mar 13 - Mar 27)

Introduction to the Gospel of John: authorship (likely Johannine school, connected to "beloved disciple"), dating (c. 90-95 CE), sophisticated Greek style, historical context (conflicts within Judaism and with Rome), high Christology. Discussion of Chapters 1-13. Key themes: *Logos* (Word) in the Prologue, creation parallels (Genesis 1, Proverbs 8), Jesus's titles (Lamb of God, Son of God, Son of Man), miracles as "signs" (*sēmeia*), knowing vs. believing, seeing vs. signs, John the Baptist as witness, being "born again/from above," the Samaritan woman (living water), Jesus's humanity and divinity, the body as temple (*kenosis*), Father-Son relationship, and apparent contradictions within the text. Focus on unique Johannine perspective compared to Synoptics.

Key Concepts & Philosophies

Advaita Vedānta (Non-Dualism)

One of the major schools of Vedānta, strongly associated with Śaṅkara. Its core teaching is *Advaita* - "not two." It asserts that the individual self (*Ātman*) and the ultimate reality (*Brahman*) are ontologically one. The perceived multiplicity of the world is ultimately illusory (*māyā*) or arises from distinctions (*ontic*) within the single reality (*ontological*). Liberation (*mokṣa*) comes through *jñāna* (knowledge/wisdom) – realizing this non-dual identity. Often seen as non-theistic in the sense of denying a separate, personal creator God distinct from Brahman/Ātman.

Analogy: The two sides of a coin are distinct (ontic difference) but belong to one coin (ontological unity). They are "not two."

Viśiṣṭādvaita Vedānta (Qualified Non-Dualism)

A major school of Vedānta, associated with Rāmānuja. It means "qualified non-dualism." It agrees that Brahman is the ultimate reality but posits that Brahman possesses real attributes and includes within itself the distinct realities of individual souls (*cit*) and matter (*acit*). These form the "body" of Brahman (God/Īśvara), who is personal and the object of devotion (*bhakti*). God is seen as both the material and efficient cause. Allows for a devotional relationship with a personal God while maintaining ultimate non-duality in a qualified sense.

Līlā (Divine Play)

Meaning "play," "sport," or "pastime." In theology, it explains divine action, especially creation, not as arising from need or lack, but as a spontaneous, joyful expression of the divine nature, like a child playing or an artist creating. It emphasizes freedom, creativity, and delight. Kṛṣṇa's activities, particularly in his youth, are seen as quintessential examples of Līlā. It is also associated with certain forms of religious drama.

Dharma

A foundational concept with multiple meanings: duty, righteousness, cosmic law, ethics, inherent nature, that which upholds order. For Mīmāṃsā, Dharma is primarily "what is to be done," established by Vedic injunctions (rituals). In the Bhagavad Gītā, it refers to one's societal duty (e.g., Arjuna's duty as a warrior, *svadharma*) as well as universal righteousness.

Karma

Meaning "action," "work," or "deed." In Mīmāṃsā, it specifically refers to Vedic ritual action (*yajña*). In broader philosophy, it includes all actions and the principle of cause and effect, where actions create consequences (*saṃskāras*) that influence future lives. The Bhagavad Gītā reinterprets Karma Yoga as the path of selfless action performed without attachment to results.

Pramāṇas (Valid Sources of Knowledge)

The means by which valid knowledge (*pramā*) is acquired in Indian philosophy. Key pramāṇas include *Pratyakṣa* (direct perception), *Anumāna* (inference), and *Śabda* (verbal testimony, especially scripture like the Vedas). The Mīmāṃsā school, particularly Kumārila Bhaṭṭa, also discussed *Abhāva* (non-existence/absence) as a distinct pramāṇa revealing causality (the cause ceases as the effect begins).

Causality

Examined through different lenses: Material Cause (the substance from which something is made, e.g., clay, Brahman), Efficient Cause (the agent that shapes or brings about the effect, e.g., the potter, God, self/world), and Final Cause (the purpose, goal, or *telos*, often identified as *Līlā*). *Satkāryavāda* is the theory that the effect pre-exists in the cause.

Śruti vs. Smṛti

Two categories of Hindu scripture. *Śruti* ("that which is heard") refers to the Vedas, considered divinely revealed and authorless (*apauruṣeya*). Interpretation often focuses on the precise meaning of every word. *Smṛti* ("that which is remembered") includes epics (Mahābhārata, Rāmāyaṇa), Purāṇas, law texts, etc., derived from tradition and considered secondary to Śruti. Interpretation often focuses more on the overall narrative and meaning.

Mahāvākyas

"Great Sayings" - four key aphorisms from the Upaniṣads considered to encapsulate the essence of Vedānta, particularly Advaita:

  1. *Ahaṃ Brahmāsmi* ("I am Brahman") - Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad
  2. *Tat Tvam Asi* ("Thou Art That") - Chāndogya Upaniṣad
  3. *Prajñānam Brahma* ("Consciousness is Brahman") - Aitareya Upaniṣad
  4. *Ayam Ātmā Brahma* ("This Self is Brahman") - Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad

Trimārga (Three Paths)

The three main paths to liberation (*mokṣa*) in Hinduism:

  • *Karma Mārga*: The path of action (often ritual action).
  • *Jñāna Mārga*: The path of knowledge/wisdom.
  • *Bhakti Mārga*: The path of devotion.

Different schools and texts may emphasize one path over others or integrate them.

Avatāra

"Descent." The concept of a deity (especially Viṣṇu) manifesting in a worldly form to restore dharma or engage in līlā. Unlike incarnation in some traditions, the avatāra is typically understood as fully divine, merely appearing in a form, not becoming limited by it.

Logos & Sophia

*Logos* (Greek: Word, Reason, Principle) is central to the Prologue of John's Gospel, describing the pre-existent, divine agent of creation. *Sophia* (Greek: Wisdom) is the translation of Hebrew *Ḥokmāh*, personified in Proverbs 8, providing a crucial link for early Greek-speaking Christians.

Imago Dei

(Latin: Image of God). The concept from Genesis 1:26-27 that humankind is created in the image and likeness of God. Discussed in contrast to the *Enūma Eliš* view of humans as slaves, highlighting the potential interpretations (physical likeness, rationality, creativity, moral capacity, relationality).

Key Comparative Points

  • Creation Narratives: Contrasting Genesis 1 (speech, order, goodness, Imago Dei) with Genesis 2-3 (hands-on, garden, prohibition), Vedic Hymns (questioning, sacrifice, unfolding), Proverbs 8 (pre-existent Wisdom), and Enūma Eliš (cosmic battle, humans as slaves).
  • Nature of God/Ultimate Reality: Exploring concepts like Elohim vs. YHWH, the Trinity, Brahman (Nirguṇa vs. Saguṇa), Ātman, the relationship between the One and Many (Advaita vs. Viśiṣṭādvaita).
  • Divine Manifestation: Comparing concepts like Avatāra (divine descent in Hinduism, e.g., Kṛṣṇa) with Incarnation (Christianity, the Word becoming flesh in John).
  • Paths to Liberation/Salvation: Examining the Trimārga (Karma, Jñāna, Bhakti) in Hinduism vs. Christian concepts of salvation through faith, grace, and works (as interpreted through John's Gospel).
  • Role of Scripture: Understanding the authority and function of Śruti vs. Smṛti in Hinduism compared to the role of the Hebrew Bible and New Testament in Christianity.
  • Ethics and Duty: Comparing the concept of Dharma with notions of divine law, commandment, and love in the Judeo-Christian tradition.
  • Knowledge vs. Devotion: The tension and synthesis between Jñāna Mārga and Bhakti Mārga finds parallels in discussions of faith vs. reason or intellectual understanding vs. relational knowing in Christianity.
