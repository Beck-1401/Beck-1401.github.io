The Religious Quest (Theology 1432) - Class Report

Synopsis

This session marked the beginning of the course "The Religious Quest (Theology 1432)". Brad opened the class with a traditional Sanskrit mantra from the Taitirīya Upaniṣad, chanting it first alone and then leading the class in recitation multiple times. They then provided a detailed line-by-line explanation of the mantra's meaning, emphasizing themes of togetherness, protection through knowledge, nourishment, heroic action, light, and avoiding animosity. Brad introduced themself, sharing their academic background (music, finance, theology), personal experiences (including being in NYC during 9/11 which prompted a life re-evaluation), studies in India (Sanskrit, Bhagavad Gītā), time at Harvard, family life, and current homesteading lifestyle. They outlined the course structure, focusing on the comparative study of Hindu and Christian sacred texts (Tuesdays for Hinduism, Thursdays for Christianity), with specific attention to creation, creativity, recreation/play (*līlā*), non-dualism (*advaita*), and ethics (*dharma*). Brad discussed Anselm's definition of theology (*fides quaerens intellectum*), the course objectives (written in Sanskrit *śloka* meter), and the importance of *saṃgraha* (summary) and *vistāra* (expansion) in studying texts. They reviewed syllabus details, including grading (attendance, participation, quizzes, leadership days, podcast discussions, final exam), policies (no laptops, absence policy), and introduced the team-based learning approach. The class included a brief small group discussion for students to get acquainted and concluded with the assignment to read Genesis 1-3 for the next session.

Vocabulary

  • Om (ॐ): A sacred syllable in Hinduism, considered primordial sound, representing ultimate reality. Discussed as encompassing all vowels and thus all possible words.
  • Saha nāv avatu (सह नाववतु): "May He protect us both together." The first line of the opening mantra.
  • Saha nau bhunaktu (सह नौ भुनक्तु): "May He nourish us both together." The second line of the mantra.
  • Saha vīryaṃ karavāvahai (सह वीर्यं करवावहै): "May we work together with energy and vigor (heroically)." The third line of the mantra.
  • Tejasvi nāv adhītam astu (तेजस्वि नावधीतमस्तु): "May our study be illuminating and fruitful." The fourth line of the mantra.
  • Mā vidviṣāvahai (मा विद्विषावहै): "May we not hate/despise each other." The fifth line of the mantra.
  • Śānti (शान्ति): Peace. Repeated three times for peace in the body, mind, and spirit/environment.
  • Taitirīya Upaniṣad (तैत्तिरीयोपनिषद्): An ancient Sanskrit text, part of the Vedas, from which the opening mantra is sourced.
  • Saha (सह): Together.
  • Nau (नौ): Us two (dual form).
  • Vavatu: Protect.
  • Bhunaktu: Nourish (from root 'bhuj' - to eat/enjoy).
  • Vīryam (वीर्यं): Heroism, strength, vigor. Related to 'vīra' (hero).
  • Kara (कर): To make, do, shape, carve. Related to hands-on action.
  • Tejas (तेजस्): Light, brilliance, splendor, spiritual power, goodness.
  • Mā (मा): Not, do not, never.
  • Vid: Related to bad vibes, hostility, negativity ("no smoke").
  • Mandukya Upaniṣad (माण्डूक्योपनिषद्): Another Upanishad, known for its exposition on Om.
  • Anusvāra (अनुस्वार): The nasal sound (ṃ) often ending Om.
  • Ādi Śaṅkarācārya (आदि शङ्कराचार्य): Influential philosopher of Advaita Vedānta, commented on the Śānti mantra.
  • fides quaerens intellectum: (Latin) "Faith seeking understanding." St. Anselm's definition of theology.
  • quaerens: (Latin) Seeking, questioning, questing.
  • Śruti (श्रुति): "That which is heard"; the revealed scriptures of Hinduism, primarily the Vedas.
  • Smṛti (स्मृति): "That which is remembered"; secondary scriptures, including epics and law books.
  • Līlā (लीला): Divine play, sport, cosmic drama. A key theme for the course.
  • Advaita (अद्वैत): Non-dualism, a major school of Vedānta philosophy.
  • Dharma (धर्म): Duty, ethics, righteousness, cosmic law, intrinsic nature.
  • Anuṣṭubh Śloka (अनुष्टुभ् श्लोक): A common Sanskrit poetic meter (used for the course objectives).
  • Ṣaḍ Darśana (षड्दर्शन): The six orthodox schools/perspectives of Hindu philosophy/theology.
  • Darśana (दर्शन): Seeing, view, perspective, philosophy.
  • Abhiṣikta (अभिषिक्त): Anointed; used as a Sanskrit translation for Christ.
  • Sṛṣṭi (सृष्टि): Creation, emission, unfolding, diversification.
  • Jñāna (ज्ञान): Knowledge, wisdom, spiritual insight.
  • Pūrva Mīmāṃsā (पूर्वमीमांसा): One of the six Darśanas, focused on Vedic ritual and interpretation.
  • Advaita Vedānta (अद्वैत वेदान्त): School of non-dualism, based on the Upanishads, associated with Śaṅkara.
  • Yoga (योग): One of the six Darśanas, focusing on discipline, meditation, and liberation.
  • Sāṅkhya (सांख्य): One of the six Darśanas, a dualistic philosophy often paired with Yoga.
  • Saṃgraha (संग्रह): Grasping together, summary, collection, condensation.
  • Vistāra (विस्तार): Spreading out, elaboration, expansion, detailed commentary.
  • Karma (कर्म): Action, work, deed, specifically Vedic ritual action. (Distinguished from popular Western interpretations).
  • Dharmaram Vidya Kshetram (धर्माराम विद्या क्षेत्रम्): Pontifical University in Bangalore, India where Brad studied.
  • Guru (गुरु): Teacher, spiritual guide.
  • Bhagavad Gītā (भगवद्गीता): "Song of the Lord," an important Hindu scripture, part of the Mahābhārata epic.

Key Takeaways

  • The course, "The Religious Quest," explores creation, creativity, and recreation/play by comparing Hindu and Christian sacred texts.
  • Class structure involves studying Hindu traditions (Śruti/Smṛti) on Tuesdays and Christian traditions (Hebrew/Greek Testaments) on Thursdays, focusing on understanding each on its own terms before potential comparison emerges naturally.
  • The opening Sanskrit mantra emphasizes collaborative learning: seeking protection, nourishment, heroic action for others, illumination, and mutual respect ("no smoke") despite differing perspectives.
  • Theology, following Anselm, is understood as *fides quaerens intellectum* (faith seeking understanding), a dynamic quest involving continuous questioning of one's understanding and assumptions.
  • Key themes include divine play (*līlā*), non-dualism (*advaita*), ethics/duty (*dharma*), and creation (*sṛṣṭi*).
  • The study of sacred texts involves both grasping the essence (*saṃgraha*) and detailed elaboration (*vistāra*).
  • The course utilizes team-based learning and podcast discussions as primary tools. Laptops are generally not permitted to encourage full engagement.
  • The instructor, Brad, brings a diverse background in music, finance, theology, comparative religion (Harvard), and practical experience (homesteading) to the course.
  • The first reading assignment is Genesis chapters 1-3 for the upcoming Thursday class.
