Advaita Vedanta: The Mahavakyas

Synopsis

This class session focused on Advaita Vedānta, one of the six orthodox schools (Ṣaḍ Darśana) of Hindu philosophy, emphasizing its core principle of non-duality (Advaita). Brad reviewed the concept and the first Mahāvākya ("Great Saying"), Aham Brahmāsmi ("I am Brahman"), from the Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad. The main focus shifted to the second Mahāvākya, Tat Tvam Asi ("Thou art That" or "You are That"), from the Chāndogya Upaniṣad. Students engaged in small group discussions, using Play-Doh to model various analogies presented in sections 8 through 16 of the text, which illustrate the relationship between the individual self (Ātman) and the ultimate reality (Brahman). Each group presented their understanding of a specific section (rivers merging into the ocean, the living tree, the Banyan seed's essence, salt dissolving in water, the blindfolded man needing a guide, the process of dying, and the ordeal by axe). Brad also introduced the third Mahāvākya, Prajñānam Brahma ("Consciousness is Brahman") from the Aitareya Upaniṣad, and previewed the fourth, Ayam Ātmā Brahma ("This Self is Brahman"), along with the upcoming reading of the Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad. The importance of reading texts comparatively and harmoniously, drawing parallels to Mīmāṁsā principles, was also highlighted.

Vocabulary

  • Advaita Vedānta (अद्वैत वेदान्त): Non-dual Vedanta philosophy; emphasizes the identity of Ātman and Brahman.
  • Aham Brahmāsmi (अहं ब्रह्मास्मि): "I am Brahman," the first Mahāvākya discussed, from the Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad.
  • Aitareya Upaniṣad (ऐतरेय उपनिषद्): Source of the third Mahāvākya, Prajñānam Brahma.
  • Ātman (आत्मन्): The Self, soul; the individual essence.
  • Ayam Ātmā Brahma (अयम् आत्मा ब्रह्म): "This Self is Brahman," the fourth Mahāvākya, from the Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad (previewed).
  • Brahman (ब्रह्मन्): The ultimate reality, the Absolute, the ground of all being.
  • Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad (बृहदारण्यक उपनिषद्): An early, major Upanishad, also considered an Āraṇyaka; source of "Aham Brahmāsmi".
  • Chāndogya Upaniṣad (छान्दोग्य उपनिषद्): Source of the second Mahāvākya, "Tat Tvam Asi". Sections 8-16 were discussed in detail.
  • Gāndhāra (गान्धार): Ancient region/people mentioned in the analogy of the blindfolded man (Section 14).
  • Jīva / Jīva Ātman (जीव / जीव आत्मन्): The living self, the individual soul, particularly as embodied (discussed in Section 11).
  • Mahāvākya (महावाक्य): "Great Saying"; profound utterances summarizing Upanishadic teachings. Four were discussed/mentioned.
  • Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad (माण्डूक्य उपनिषद्): A short Upanishad focusing on states of consciousness; source of "Ayam Ātmā Brahma" (assigned reading).
  • Mīmāṁsā (मीमांसा): One of the six orthodox schools (Ṣaḍ Darśana), focused on hermeneutics and ritual; mentioned for its emphasis on consistent scriptural interpretation.
  • Nyagrodha (न्यग्रोध): The Banyan tree, used in an analogy about hidden essence (Section 12).
  • Prajñānam Brahma (प्रज्ञानं ब्रह्म): "Consciousness is Brahman," the third Mahāvākya, from the Aitareya Upaniṣad.
  • Prāṇa (प्राण): Breath, life force; vital energy.
  • Ṣaḍ Darśana (षड्दर्शन): The six orthodox schools/perspectives of Hindu philosophy (Vedānta is one).
  • Sat (सत्): Being, existence, reality, truth. Discussed in relation to sleep (Section 8) and the ordeal (Section 16).
  • Satya (सत्य): Truth; derived from Sat.
  • Śvetaketu (श्वेतकेतु): The son receiving teachings from his father Uddālaka Āruṇi in the Chāndogya Upaniṣad.
  • Tat Tvam Asi (तत् त्वम् असि): "Thou art That" or "You are That," the second Mahāvākya discussed, repeated in Chāndogya Upaniṣad sections 8-16.
  • Tejas (तेजस्): Heat, fire, brilliance; one of the elements into which the faculties merge at death (Section 15).
  • Vedānta (वेदान्त): "End of the Vedas"; one of the Ṣaḍ Darśana, focusing on the philosophical teachings of the Upanishads.

Key Takeaways

  • Advaita Vedānta posits non-duality: the ultimate reality (Brahman) and the individual self (Ātman) are fundamentally one and the same.
  • The Mahāvākyas ("Great Sayings") encapsulate this core teaching (e.g., "Tat Tvam Asi" - You are That; "Aham Brahmāsmi" - I am Brahman).
  • The Chāndogya Upaniṣad employs numerous analogies (rivers/ocean, tree/life-force, Banyan seed/essence, salt/water) to illustrate that the subtle, unperceived essence (Brahman/Ātman) underlies all existence and is identical to the individual self.
  • True understanding involves recognizing this underlying reality (Sat) which is not immediately apparent through the senses.
  • Spiritual guidance from a teacher (guru) is often necessary to navigate the path to self-realization, as illustrated by the analogy of the blindfolded man from Gāndhāra.
  • Vedānta emphasizes reading scriptures holistically and harmoniously, ensuring consistent interpretation (a principle shared with the Mīmāṁsā school).
  • Concepts like Prāṇa (life force/breath), Sat (being/truth), and the stages of consciousness/dissolution at death are integral to understanding the relationship between the individual and the absolute.

But if he has not committed the theft, then he makes himself true. Being given to truth (Satya), he covers himself with truth, and seizing the heated axe, he is not burnt, and then he is released. As that truthful man is not burnt, thus has all that exists that truth (Sat) as its self. That is the truth, that is the self. Tat Tvam Asi, you are that, Śvetaketu.

Concluding Remarks

Brad: And then he understood. Good. Excellent.
