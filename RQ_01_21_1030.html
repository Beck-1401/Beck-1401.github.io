Class Report: Introduction to Hindu Traditions and Vedic Hymns

Synopsis

This class session served as an introduction to Hindu traditions, emphasizing the importance of specific terminology and avoiding the generalized term "Hinduism." Brad began with a moment of quiet and a chant from the Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad. They then outlined the foundational canon of Hindu theology, the śruti ("that which is heard"), focusing on the Vedas. The structure of the Vedas (Ṛg, Sāma, Yajur, Atharva) and their fourfold divisions (Saṃhitā, Brāhmaṇa, Āraṇyaka, Upaniṣad) were explained. Brad introduced the six orthodox schools of thought (Ṣaḍ-darśana), providing a deeper look into the Mīmāṃsā school. Key concepts discussed included the Mīmāṃsā focus on ritual performance derived from the Brāhmaṇas, the idea of scripture as arthavāda (incentive/motivation for action), the concept of dharma as something to be brought into being through action (karma), and the phrase śabda mātraṃ devatā ("God is nothing but a word"). The latter half of the class involved a group reading and discussion of three hymns from the ṚgVeda Saṃhitā: the Nāsadīya Sūkta (X.129), the Hiraṇyagarbha Sūkta (X.121), and the Puruṣa Sūkta (X.90), exploring themes of creation, questioning, sacrifice, and the nature of reality from various perspectives, including the Mīmāṃsā lens.

Vocabulary

  • Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad (बृहदारण्यक उपनिषद्) - One of the major, older Upanishads; source of the opening chant.
  • Om (ॐ) - A sacred syllable and mantra.
  • Asato mā sadgamaya (असतो मा सद्गमय) - "From the unreal lead me to the real." (Part of the chant).
  • Tamaso mā jyotir gamaya (तमसो मा ज्योतिर् गमय) - "From darkness lead me to light." (Part of the chant).
  • Mṛtyor mā amṛtaṃ gamaya (मृत्योर् मा अमृतं गमय) - "From death lead me to immortality." (Part of the chant).
  • Śānti (शान्ति) - Peace (chanted three times).
  • śruti (श्रुति) - "That which is heard"; the revealed, authorless canon of Hindu scripture (primarily the Vedas). Contrasted with written scripture or humanly composed texts.
  • Ṛṣi (ऋषि) - Seer or perceiver; ancient sages who 'heard' or perceived the śruti.
  • Veda (वेद) - "Knowledge"; the collection of texts forming the śruti, considered sacred knowledge. Root *vid* means "to know."
  • Ṛg Veda (ऋग्वेद) - The first of the four Vedas, primarily composed of hymns (Saṃhitās).
  • Sāma Veda (सामवेद) - The second Veda, consisting mainly of melodies and chants derived from the Ṛg Veda.
  • Yajur Veda (यजुर्वेद) - The third Veda, containing prose mantras and formulas for ritual sacrifice (yajña).
  • Atharva Veda (अथर्ववेद) - The fourth Veda, containing hymns, spells, and incantations.
  • Saṃhitā (संहिता) - "Collection"; the first layer of each Veda, primarily hymns or mantras.
  • Brāhmaṇa (ब्राह्मण) - The second layer; prose texts explaining the rituals and myths associated with the Saṃhitās. Focus of the Mīmāṃsā school.
  • Āraṇyaka (आरण्यक) - "Forest texts"; the third layer, containing more philosophical reflections on rituals, often studied in seclusion.
  • Upaniṣad (उपनिषद्) - "Sitting near devotedly"; the fourth and final layer, containing philosophical and mystical teachings; forms the basis of Vedānta.
  • Vedānta (वेदान्त) - "End of the Vedas" (anta = end); refers both to the Upanishads as the final part of the Vedic corpus and the philosophical schools based on them. "End" implies culmination or ultimate goal.
  • Ṣaḍ-darśana (षड्दर्शन) - "Six perspectives" or "six viewpoints" (ṣaḍ = six, darśana = seeing/perspective); the six orthodox schools of Hindu philosophy (Mīmāṃsā, Vedānta, Nyāya, Vaiśeṣika, Sāṃkhya, Yoga).
  • Mīmāṃsā (मीमांसा) - One of the six darśanas, focused on the correct interpretation (hermeneutics) of the Vedas, particularly the Brāhmaṇas, to ensure proper ritual performance (yajña/karma) for upholding dharma.
  • Nyāya (न्याय) - A darśana focused on logic and epistemology.
  • Vaiśeṣika (वैशेषिक) - A darśana often paired with Nyāya, focused on metaphysics and atomism.
  • Sāṃkhya (सांख्य) - A dualistic darśana focused on the relationship between consciousness (puruṣa) and matter (prakṛti).
  • Yoga (योग) - A darśana often paired with Sāṃkhya, focused on meditative and physical practices for liberation.
  • śabda mātraṃ devatā (शब्द मात्रं देवता) - "God (devatā) is nothing but (mātraṃ) a word (śabda)"; a Mīmāṃsā phrase emphasizing that the names of deities in rituals are primarily important for their correct pronunciation within the ritual context, not necessarily for belief in their independent existence.
  • Devatā (देवता) / Deva (देव) - Deity; in Mīmāṃsā context, the recipient of a sacrifice, whose name must be uttered correctly. Derived from the dative case ("to whom it is given").
  • yajña (यज्ञ) - Sacrifice; the central ritual practice discussed in the Brāhmaṇas, typically involving offerings into a fire.
  • karma (कर्म) - Action; often refers specifically to ritual action in the Mīmāṃsā context.
  • arthavāda (अर्थवाद) - Statements in scripture that are not direct injunctions for ritual but serve as motivation, praise, or explanation to encourage ritual performance. Mīmāṃsā interprets non-injunctive parts of the Veda (like hymns or Upanishadic stories) as arthavāda.
  • pratyakṣa (प्रत्यक्ष) - Direct perception; one of the means of valid knowledge (pramāṇa). Mīmāṃsā argues scripture doesn't tell us what perception already does.
  • itikartavyatā (इतिकर्तव्यता) - "The way it is supposed to be done"; refers to the specific procedures and details of ritual performance, a key concern for Mīmāṃsā.
  • dharma (धर्म) - A complex term with no single English equivalent; refers to duty, righteousness, cosmic order, law, virtue, the inherent nature of things. For Mīmāṃsā, dharma is primarily established and maintained through Vedic injunctions (rituals) and is something to be brought into being through action.
  • adharma (अधर्म) - The opposite of dharma; unrighteousness, disorder, wrong action.
  • Nāsadīya Sūkta (नासदीय सूक्त) - "Hymn of Non-existence"; ṚgVeda X.129, a famous hymn speculating on creation from a state beyond being and non-being.
  • Hiraṇyagarbha (हिरण्यगर्भ) - "Golden Embryo" or "Golden Womb"; ṚgVeda X.121, a hymn describing creation emerging from a golden seed/embryo, often associated with Prajāpati or the sun.
  • Puruṣa Sūkta (पुरुष सूक्त) - "Hymn of the Person"; ṚgVeda X.90, describing the creation of the cosmos through the sacrifice of a primeval cosmic being (Puruṣa).
  • Puruṣa (पुरुष) - Person, cosmic being, consciousness. In the Puruṣa Sūkta, the primordial being whose sacrifice creates the universe. Also a key concept in Sāṃkhya. (Grammatically neuter or masculine depending on context, but here referring to the cosmic entity).
  • Indra (इन्द्र) - King of the gods in the Vedic pantheon, associated with thunder, rain, and warfare. Mentioned in the Puruṣa Sūkta.
  • Agni (अग्नि) - God of fire, central to Vedic ritual as the intermediary between humans and gods. Mentioned in the Puruṣa Sūkta.
  • Sādhya (साध्य) - A class of celestial beings or ancient gods mentioned in the Puruṣa Sūkta.
  • Dakṣa (दक्ष) - Often means "skillful" or "able"; also a creator deity, sometimes identified with Prajāpati or related to Aditi. Mentioned in a translation of Hiraṇyagarbha Sūkta.
  • Prajāpati (प्रजापति) - "Lord of Creatures"; a creator deity often identified with Hiraṇyagarbha. (Pati = lord/master, potentially related to 'father').
  • Virāj (विराज्) - The feminine principle of creation, sometimes seen as emerging from or identical with Puruṣa, representing the manifest universe. Mentioned in the Puruṣa Sūkta.

Key Takeaways

  • Hindu theology is grounded in a canon called śruti ("that which is heard"), primarily the Vedas, considered authorless and directly perceived by ancient ṛṣis.
  • The Vedas are divided into four main collections (Ṛg, Sāma, Yajur, Atharva) and four layers within each (Saṃhitā, Brāhmaṇa, Āraṇyaka, Upaniṣad).
  • The Ṣaḍ-darśana are the six orthodox perspectives or schools of thought interpreting the Vedic canon.
  • The Mīmāṃsā school focuses on interpreting the Vedas (especially Brāhmaṇas) to ensure correct ritual performance (yajña/karma).
  • Mīmāṃsā views non-injunctive parts of scripture (hymns, stories) as arthavāda – material intended to motivate the performance of rituals.
  • For Mīmāṃsā, scripture's primary purpose is prescriptive (telling us what actions to perform), not descriptive (telling us about existing reality, which is known through perception and inference). Its goal is to guide action (karma) to create and uphold dharma.
  • Deities (Devas) in the Mīmāṃsā ritual context are primarily significant as the recipients named in the mantras ("God is nothing but a word" - śabda mātraṃ devatā); correct pronunciation is key.
  • Dharma is understood as righteousness or cosmic order that does not inherently exist but must be actively brought into being through prescribed actions (rituals).
  • The Nāsadīya Sūkta (ṚgVeda X.129) highlights radical questioning about origins, emphasizing mystery and the limits of knowledge, even for the gods or a potential creator. It explores creation emerging from undifferentiated unity beyond being and non-being.
  • The Hiraṇyagarbha Sūkta (ṚgVeda X.121) uses the metaphor of a "Golden Embryo" (egg yolk, sun, seed) to describe creation unfolding from a single, undifferentiated source, posing the recurring question: "What God shall we adore with our oblation?"
  • The Puruṣa Sūkta (ṚgVeda X.90) presents creation as a cosmic sacrifice of the primordial Person (Puruṣa), from whose dismembered body all aspects of the universe (including social order, natural elements, and gods) emerge. This reinforces the centrality of sacrifice (yajña) in the Vedic worldview.
  • Studying scriptures often requires engaging with the original language or multiple translations to grasp nuances lost in translation.
