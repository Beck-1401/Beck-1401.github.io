Class Report: Introduction to Advaita Vedānta and the Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad

Synopsis

This class session began with a meditative silence followed by the chanting and explanation of the *Asato mā sadgamaya* mantra from the *Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad*. After taking attendance, Brad reviewed the concept of *Ṣaḍ Darśana* (the six orthodox schools of Hindu philosophy), focusing on *Mīmāṃsā* and its emphasis on ritual and hermeneutics. They then introduced the topic for the new unit: *Vedānta* (also referred to as *Uttara Mīmāṃsā*), highlighting its close relationship with *Mīmāṃsā* but its focus on the *Upaniṣads* (theological texts). Brad distinguished between two major schools within *Vedānta*: the non-theistic *Advaita* (non-dualism) and the theistic *Viśiṣṭādvaita* (qualified non-dualism). The session focused primarily on *Advaita Vedānta*, introducing the concept of the *Mahāvākyas* (Great Utterances) found in specific *Upaniṣads*. The class then engaged in a collective reading and detailed discussion of a passage from the *Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad* (1.4.1-10), which contains the *Mahāvākya* *Aham Brahmāsmi* ("I am Brahman"). Key themes explored included the origin of the universe from a single Self (*Ātman*), the motivation for creation (overcoming loneliness, play), the nature of the Self, the concept of creation as unfolding (*śṛṣṭi*) rather than division, the meaning of "I" (*Aham*), the relationship between *Ātman* and *Brahman*, different types of causality, and the *Tri Mārga* (three paths to liberation: *Karma*, *Jñāna*, *Bhakti*). The discussion compared the creation narrative to other texts like Genesis, *Puruṣa Sūkta*, and *Nāsadīya Sūkta*.

Key Takeaways

  • The *Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad* is a foundational text for *Vedānta*, particularly *Advaita Vedānta*.
  • The mantra *Asato mā sadgamaya* encapsulates a movement from non-being/untruth to being/truth, darkness to light, and death to immortality.
  • *Vedānta* (or *Uttara Mīmāṃsā*) builds upon the hermeneutical principles of *Mīmāṃsā* but applies them to the philosophical/theological content of the *Upaniṣads*.
  • *Advaita Vedānta* posits non-duality ("not two"), asserting the ultimate identity of the individual Self (*Ātman*) and the ultimate reality (*Brahman*). It is generally non-theistic.
  • *Viśiṣṭādvaita Vedānta* posits qualified non-duality, maintaining the ultimate reality of Brahman but acknowledging real distinctions (qualifications) between God, souls, and matter. It is theistic.
  • The *Mahāvākyas* ("Great Utterances") are key sentences from the Upaniṣads summarizing Advaita teachings. *Aham Brahmāsmi* ("I am Brahman") from the *Bṛhadāraṇyaka* is one such utterance.
  • The creation narrative in *Bṛhadāraṇyaka* 1.4 depicts the universe originating from a single Self (*Ātman* in the form of a *Puruṣa*) who, out of loneliness and lack of pleasure, desired companionship and became the multiplicity of the world.
  • Creation is understood not as division, but as *śṛṣṭi* (unfolding or manifestation), where the One becomes Many without ceasing to be One, like unfolding a piece of paper.
  • The pronoun "I" (*Aham*), when understood correctly, refers to this single, universal Self (*Ātman*) that underlies all individual beings.
  • Realizing *Aham Brahmāsmi* ("I am Brahman") is the core of *Jñāna* (knowledge) in Advaita, leading to liberation (*mokṣa*).
  • The purpose of creation can be understood as overcoming the boredom/loneliness of oneness, or as divine play (*Līlā*).
  • The *Tri Mārga* (*Karma*, *Jñāna*, *Bhakti*) represent three valid paths to liberation, though some theologians (like Shankara) prioritize *Jñāna Mārga*.
  • Existence involves a continuous process of transformation (folding and unfolding), where beings are both eaters and eaten, all manifestations of the one Brahman/Ātman.
