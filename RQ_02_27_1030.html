Vocabulary

  • Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad (बृहदारण्यक उपनिषद्) - An important Upanishad, source of the Mahāvākya "Aham Brahmāsmi".
  • Aham Brahmāsmi (अहम् ब्रह्मास्मि) - "I am Brahman." One of the four Mahāvākyas (Great Sayings).
  • Chāndogya Upaniṣad (छान्दोग्य उपनिषद्) - An important Upanishad, source of the Mahāvākya "Tat Tvam Asi".
  • Tat Tvam Asi (तत् त्वम् असि) - "Thou art That" or "That you are." One of the four Mahāvākyas.
  • Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad (माण्डूक्य उपनिषद्) - An important Upanishad, source of the Mahāvākya "Ayam Ātmā Brahma".
  • Ayam Ātmā Brahma (अयम् आत्मा ब्रह्म) - "This Self (Ātman) is Brahman." One of the four Mahāvākyas.
  • Aitareya Upaniṣad (ऐतरेय उपनिषद्) - An important Upanishad, source of the Mahāvākya "Prajñānam Brahma".
  • Prajñānam Brahma (प्रज्ञानम् ब्रह्म) - "Consciousness is Brahman." One of the four Mahāvākyas.
  • Yaśodā (यशोदा) - Krishna's foster mother.
  • Rāsa Līlā (रास लीला) - The divine dance performed by Krishna with the Gopis (cow-herd girls).
  • via negativa - Latin phrase meaning "negative way," referring to approaching God by describing what God is *not*. Corresponds to Apophatic theology and Nirguṇa Brahman.
  • nirguṇa Brahman (निर्गुण ब्रह्मन्) - Brahman without qualities; the impersonal, formless Absolute Reality.
  • apophatic (ἀποφατικός) - Greek term for theology that speaks of God by negation (what God is not).
  • Ṣaḍ Darśana (षड् दर्शन) - The six orthodox schools (perspectives) of Hindu philosophy.
  • Mīmāṃsā (मीमांसा) - One of the six darśanas, focused on the exegesis of the Vedas.
  • Vedānta (वेदान्त) - One of the six darśanas, focused on the philosophy of the Upanishads ("end of the Vedas").
  • Vaiśeṣika (वैशेषिक) - One of the six darśanas, known for its atomistic metaphysics.
  • Nyāya (न्याय) - One of the six darśanas, known for its system of logic and epistemology.
  • Bāla Kṛṣṇa (बाल कृष्ण) - Krishna in his infant/child form.
  • Makhan Chor (माखन चोर) - "Butter thief," an affectionate name for young Krishna.
  • Bhāgavata Purāṇa (भागवत पुराण) - A major Purāṇa detailing the avatars of Vishnu, especially the life of Krishna. Also known as Śrīmad Bhāgavatam.
  • via positiva - Latin phrase meaning "positive way," referring to approaching God through positive affirmations and descriptions. Corresponds to Cataphatic theology and Saguṇa Brahman.
  • saguṇa Brahman (सगुण ब्रह्मन्) - Brahman with qualities; the personal aspect of the Absolute Reality, often conceived as a specific deity.
  • cataphatic (καταφατικός) - Greek term for theology that speaks of God through positive attributes and affirmations.
  • Brahman (ब्रह्मन्) - The ultimate, absolute reality in Hinduism. Can be conceived as personal (saguṇa) or impersonal (nirguṇa).
  • mārga (मार्ग) - Path or way, often referring to the paths of liberation (e.g., Bhakti Mārga, Jñāna Mārga, Karma Mārga). Used interchangeably with Yoga in the Bhagavad Gītā.
  • yoga (योग) - Union; discipline or path towards liberation.
  • Bhagavad Gītā (भगवद् गीता) - "Song of the Lord," a key Hindu scripture embedded within the Mahābhārata.
  • Bhakti Mārga (भक्ति मार्ग) - The path of devotion.
  • Śruti (श्रुति) - "That which is heard"; the most authoritative category of Hindu sacred texts, primarily the Vedas.
  • Veda (वेद) - The oldest and most sacred collection of Hindu scriptures, considered Śruti.
  • Upaniṣad (उपनिषद्) - Philosophical dialogues forming the latter part of the Vedas, also known as Vedānta.
  • Vaiṣṇavism (वैष्णवधर्म) - The largest sect of Bhakti, focusing devotion on Vishnu and his avatars like Krishna and Rāma.
  • Kṛṣṇa (कृष्ण) - An avatar of Vishnu, a central deity in Vaiṣṇavism.
  • Rāma (राम) - An avatar of Vishnu, the hero of the Rāmāyaṇa.
  • theophany (θεοφάνεια) - A visible manifestation of a deity to humans (e.g., Yaśodā seeing the universe in Krishna's mouth).
  • Śāktism (शाक्तधर्म) - The second largest sect of Bhakti, focusing devotion on the Goddess (Śakti/Devī) in her various forms.
  • Mahābhārata (महाभारत) - One of the two major Sanskrit epics of ancient India (the other being the Rāmāyaṇa).
  • Sītā (सीता) - Wife of Rāma, a central figure in the Rāmāyaṇa.
  • Rāvaṇa (रावण) - The ten-headed demon king who abducts Sītā in the Rāmāyaṇa.
  • Rāmāyaṇa (रामायण) - One of the two major Sanskrit epics, telling the story of Rāma.
  • Līlā (लीला) - Divine play or sport; a concept explaining the universe as a playful creation of the divine.
  • Advaita Vedānta (अद्वैत वेदान्त) - A school of Vedānta philosophy emphasizing non-dualism (the identity of Ātman and Brahman).

Key Takeaways

  • Precise terminology is crucial in discussing philosophical and theological concepts (e.g., distinguishing Nirguṇa and Saguṇa Brahman, knowing the specific Mahāvākyas and their sources).
  • The four Mahāvākyas (Great Sayings) from the Upanishads encapsulate core Vedāntic insights about the identity of the individual self (Ātman) and ultimate reality (Brahman).
  • Krishna's life stories (Līlā), found in texts like the Bhāgavata Purāṇa, illustrate key aspects of Bhakti, including divine playfulness, love, and manifestations (theophanies).
  • Hindu philosophy includes diverse schools (Ṣaḍ Darśana), such as Nyāya (logic) and Vedānta (Upanishadic philosophy).
  • Bhakti Mārga (the path of devotion) is a major path to liberation, with large sects like Vaiṣṇavism (devotion to Vishnu/Krishna/Rama) and Śāktism (devotion to the Goddess).
  • Theological approaches can be categorized using terms like apophatic (via negativa, describing what God is *not*, related to Nirguṇa Brahman) and cataphatic (via positiva, describing what God *is*, related to Saguṇa Brahman).
  • Key Hindu texts include the Vedas (Śruti), Upanishads (Vedānta), the Epics (Rāmāyaṇa, Mahābhārata), and the Purāṇas (like the Bhāgavata Purāṇa).
  • The concept of Līlā (divine play) is central to understanding certain Hindu perspectives on creation and divine activity.
