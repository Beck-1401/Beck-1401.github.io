Advaita Vedanta: Lecture on the Mandukya Upanishad

Synopsis

This lecture concludes a unit on Advaita Vedanta, focusing on the fourth and final Mahāvākya presented through the Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad. Brad leads the class through a reading of the text, which explores the syllable Om (ॐ) and its correspondence to the four states of consciousness: waking (Vaiśvānara), dream (Taijasa), deep sleep (Prajñā), and the fourth state (Turiya), which represents the ultimate, non-dual reality (Brahman/Ātman). The lecture emphasizes interpreting the Upanishads synchronistically (Upasaṃhāra and Samanvaya). A significant portion of the class involves a question-and-answer session where students grapple with concepts like the silence following Om, the relationship between the three sounds of Om and the four states, the meaning of "lump of consciousness" (Prajñāna Ghana), the nature of the fourth state, the distinction between knower and known, and the implications of non-dualism for understanding the self and reality. Brad uses analogies like Play-Doh, a coin, and mathematical infinity to illustrate these complex philosophical ideas.

Key Takeaways

  • The Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad analyzes the syllable Om (A-U-M-Silence) as corresponding to the four states of consciousness: Waking (A), Dream (U), Deep Sleep (M), and the Fourth/Turiya (Silence).
  • The fourth state (Turiya) transcends the other three and represents the true nature of the Self (Ātman), which is non-dual (Advaita) and identical to the ultimate reality (Brahman).
  • The state of deep sleep (Prajñā) is described as a "lump of consciousness" (Prajñāna Ghana), signifying undifferentiated potentiality from which manifest experiences arise, similar to how clay is the potential for various pots.
  • Understanding Advaita involves recognizing the distinction between the knower and the known is necessary for experience but ultimately illusory from an ontological perspective (like the two sides of one coin).
  • Interpretive principles like Upasaṃhāra (coordination) and Samanvaya (harmonization) are crucial for understanding Vedāntic texts as a coherent whole, connecting different Upanishads and even Vedic hymns.
  • The goal is not to erase individual particularity but to understand it as a unique manifestation ("fold") of the single, underlying Self (Ātman/Brahman), transcending objectification.
  • Silence, both as the pause between sounds and as the state beyond A-U-M, points towards the ineffable nature of the ultimate reality (Turiya/Brahman).
