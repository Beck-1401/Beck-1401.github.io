Class Report: Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad and Advaita Vedānta

Synopsis

This class session began with a meditative mantra from the Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad, followed by attendance. Brad reviewed the concept of Ṣaḍdarśana (six perspectives of orthodox Hindu thought), focusing on the relationship between Mīmāṃsā (ritual-focused hermeneutics) and Vedānta (knowledge-focused, dealing with the Upaniṣads). They introduced Vedānta as Uttara Mīmāṃsā, emphasizing its roots in Mīmāṃsā's interpretive methods. The discussion highlighted the historical context of the Upaniṣads as oral traditions later written down during periods of prosperity. Brad introduced the two major schools of Vedānta: Advaita (non-dualism) and Viśiṣṭādvaita (qualified non-dualism), noting the semester's focus on Advaita initially. The concept of the four Mahāvākyas (Great Sentences) was introduced, with the class focusing on the first, "Ahaṃ Brahmāsmi," from the Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad. The class then collectively read and discussed a passage from this Upaniṣad, exploring its creation narrative (the single Puruṣa dividing itself due to loneliness), the concept of causality, the identification of the individual self (Aham) with the ultimate reality (Brahman), and the implications for understanding unity, diversity, and the path of knowledge (Jñāna Mārga) versus devotion (Bhakti Mārga).

Key Takeaways

  • The mantra "Asato mā sad gamaya..." encapsulates a core spiritual aspiration: moving from illusion/non-being to reality/truth, from darkness to light, and from mortality to immortality/fullness of life.
  • Vedānta ("end of the Veda") focuses on the philosophical teachings of the Upaniṣads and evolved from the rigorous interpretive methods of Mīmāṃsā (which focused on Vedic rituals).
  • Advaita Vedānta teaches non-dualism – the ultimate reality is "not two"; the individual self (Ātman) is identical to the ultimate reality (Brahman). Viśiṣṭādvaita modifies this, positing a "qualified non-dualism."
  • The Mahāvākyas are four core sentences summarizing Vedāntic truth. The first, from the Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad, is "Ahaṃ Brahmāsmi" ("I am Brahman").
  • The creation story in the Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad depicts the universe originating from a single Self (Puruṣa) that divided itself into multiplicity out of loneliness, suggesting diversity is inherent and purposeful.
  • This narrative implies that all beings share the same essential substance/nature derived from the original Puruṣa/Brahman.
  • Understanding this fundamental unity ("I am Brahman") is presented as the path of knowledge (Jñāna Mārga), which can lead to liberation (Mokṣa).
  • The text suggests that realizing one's identity with Brahman may render devotion (Bhakti Mārga) to separate deities unnecessary or even based on incomplete understanding, as one realizes they *are* the Self of the gods.
  • Conflict and suffering arise from ignorance (avidyā) of this underlying unity and the true nature of the self.
  • Nothing comes from nothing (ex nihilo nihil fit); the universe is an unfolding or transformation of the pre-existing reality (Brahman/Puruṣa), which serves as its material cause.
