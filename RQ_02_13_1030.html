Class Report: Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad and Non-Duality

Synopsis

This class session focused on the Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad, concluding a short unit on the Mahāvākyas. The session began with attendance and a brief meditation using a singing bowl. The class then collectively read Brad Bannon's translation of the Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad, with Brad providing the pronunciation for Sanskrit terms. A significant portion of the class involved discussion, starting with student questions about the nature of the "fourth state" (Turīya) and its relation to the other three states (waking, dream, deep sleep). Brad explained the concept of Turīya, emphasizing its negative description in the text and its meaning as simply "the fourth." The discussion explored the relationship between Om, Brahman, and the Self (Ātman), using analogies like different names for a person (Brad/Dad/Son) and the finger pointing to the moon to illustrate how words act as signifiers pointing towards reality without being the reality itself. Key concepts like cataphasis (positive description) and apophasis (negative description), ontic distinctions versus ontological unity, and non-duality (Advaita) were explored, drawing connections to the Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad, the Nasadiya Sukta, Nicholas of Cusa, and the Gospel of John (specifically the encounter with the Samaritan woman). The class discussed how Advaita aims not to erase individuality but to see past the conceptual barriers separating individuals from the ultimate reality (Brahman), recognizing both the universal divinity and the irreducible particularity of each being.

Vocabulary

  • Mahāvākyas: The "Great Sayings" of the Upaniṣads, encapsulating core Vedāntic truths.
  • Om (ॐ): The sacred syllable, representing the totality of existence, Brahman, and the Self. Discussed as encompassing all vowels and thus the potentiality of all words/sounds.
  • Brahman (ब्रह्मन्): The ultimate reality, the Absolute, the ground of all being. Discussed as a signifier pointing to this reality.
  • Ātman (आत्मन्): The Self, individual consciousness, which in Advaita Vedānta is ultimately identical with Brahman.
  • Vaiśvānara (वैश्वानर): The first quarter/state of consciousness – the waking state, conscious of the external world. Associated with the letter 'A' of Om.
  • Taijasa (तैजस): The second quarter/state – the dream state, conscious of the internal/subtle world. Associated with the letter 'U' of Om.
  • Prājña (प्राज्ञ): The third quarter/state – the deep sleep state, a unified mass/lump of consciousness (Prajñāna Ghana), blissful. Associated with the letter 'M' of Om.
  • Turīya (तुरीय): The fourth state, beyond the other three. Described negatively (neti neti - not this, not this), representing non-dual (Advaita), peaceful, auspicious reality. It simply means "the fourth."
  • Āpti (आप्ति): Obtaining, attainment (related to 'A' of Om).
  • Ādimatva (आदिमत्त्व): Being the first (related to 'A' of Om).
  • Utkarṣa (उत्कर्ष): Raising upwards, exaltation (related to 'U' of Om).
  • Ubhayatva (उभयत्व): Equanimity, being of both (related to 'U' of Om).
  • Miti (मिति): Measuring (related to 'M' of Om).
  • Apīti (अपीति): Merging, dissolution (related to 'M' of Om).
  • Prajñāna Ghana (प्रज्ञान घन): A mass or lump of consciousness, describing the state of deep sleep (Prājña).
  • Advaita (अद्वैत): Non-duality, the core philosophy of Advaita Vedānta, asserting the ultimate identity of Ātman and Brahman. Discussed as "not two," distinct from "one."
  • Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad (बृहदारण्यक उपनिषद्): One of the oldest and most important Upaniṣads, referenced regarding the creation story (1.4) where the One became many out of loneliness/desire.
  • Nasadiya Sukta (नासदीय सूक्त): The Hymn of Creation from the ṚgVeda (X.129), referenced for its questioning of ultimate origins and knowledge ("maybe even he does not know").
  • Puruṣa (पुरुष): Cosmic Person or Principle, referenced in the context of the One dividing itself to become the many.
  • Cataphasis: Affirmative theology or description; describing something by what it *is*.
  • Apophasis: Negative theology or description; describing something by what it *is not*.

Key Takeaways

  • The Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad analyzes the syllable Om in relation to four states of consciousness: waking (Vaiśvānara), dream (Taijasa), deep sleep (Prājña), and the fourth state (Turīya).
  • Om is presented as the entirety of existence ("all this") and identical to the Self (Ātman), which is identical to Brahman.
  • The first three states correspond to the letters A, U, and M of Om, respectively, while Turīya corresponds to the syllable as a whole, including the silence, and is measureless.
  • Turīya, the fourth state, is described primarily through negation (apophasis) – it is not conscious of internal or external, not conscious, not unconscious, unseen, ungrasped, etc. It represents the non-dual (Advaita) reality.
  • Words like "Om," "Brahman," and "Ātman" are signifiers pointing towards ultimate reality, but should not be mistaken for the reality itself (like confusing the finger pointing at the moon for the moon).
  • Knowledge requires a distinction between the knower and the known. In the non-dual state of Turīya, these distinctions collapse, hence it is "unknowable" in the conventional sense.
  • Ontic distinctions (like top/bottom, inside/outside, knower/known) are necessary for phenomenal experience but are ultimately dependent on an underlying ontological unity. They are "not two" (Advaita) – distinct but inseparable and mutually constitutive.
  • Advaita Vedānta seeks to understand the non-dual reality, not by annihilating individual distinctions, but by seeing *past* the conceptual boundaries to recognize the underlying unity (Brahman) within all particulars.
  • There are parallels between this Vedāntic understanding and concepts in other traditions, such as Nicholas of Cusa's ideas and the way Christ is depicted as seeing beyond social categories in John 4.
