Pramāṇa 1: Pratyakṣa (Perception)

[00:31:50] Brad: The second Pramāṇa is Anumāna, or inference. This is knowledge derived through reasoning, based on a perceived mark (linga) and the knowledge of an invariable concomitance (vyāpti) between the mark and the inferred property.

[00:38:20] Brad: Third is Upamāna, knowledge gained through comparison or analogy. This is often used when learning about something unfamiliar by relating it to something familiar.

[00:50:00] Brad: The fifth Pramāṇa is Arthāpatti, often translated as postulation, presumption, or implication. It's gaining knowledge by assuming a fact to make sense of a perceived phenomenon that would otherwise be inexplicable.

[00:51:15] Brad: The standard example: Devadatta is alive, but he is not seen in his house. This situation leads us to postulate or presume that Devadatta must be outside his house. The postulation (being outside) is necessary to reconcile the two known facts (he's alive, he's not in the house).

[00:53:00] Brad: It differs from inference because it's not based on invariable concomitance but on explaining an apparent contradiction.

Pramāṇa 6: Anupalabdhi / Abhāva (Non-perception)

[00:55:40] Brad: Finally, the sixth Pramāṇa, accepted by the Bhāṭṭa Mīmāṃsā and Advaita schools, is Anupalabdhi or Abhāva, meaning non-perception or knowledge of absence.
