Class Discussion: Bhagavad Gita Chapters 1 & 2

Synopsis

This class session began with a brief meditation led by Brad. Following the meditation, the designated discussion leaders initiated the class activity with a game of Jenga. The main focus then shifted to a detailed discussion of the first two chapters of the Bhagavad Gita. Students shared their reactions, particularly surprise at Krishna's justification for battle, and explored themes such as the immortality of the soul versus the temporary nature of the body, the concept of duty (*dharma*), the potential conflict between *dharma* and playfulness (*lila*), the importance of acting without attachment to results, and the nature of a 'sage'. Connections were made to Kantian ethics (categorical imperative) and previous discussions on the Upanishads (e.g., *Chandogya Upanishad*). Brad elaborated on these points, introducing the chariot allegory from the *Katha Upanishad* as a framework for interpreting the Gita psychologically, discussed Mahatma Gandhi's interpretation of the text as a call to non-violent action against injustice, highlighted structural elements like poetic meter, and explained the term *Brahman Nirvana* from the end of Chapter 2. The session concluded with brief announcements and a post-class question.

Vocabulary

  • Hari Om (हरि ओम्): A sacred Sanskrit mantra or invocation, often used at the beginning or end of prayers or meditation. Mentioned by Brad after the meditation.
  • Lila (लीला): Sanskrit term meaning 'play', 'sport', or 'pastime'. Often refers to the cosmic play of the divine or the playful nature of reality. Discussed in relation to enjoyment and the conflict with duty.
  • Dharma (धर्म): A core concept in Hinduism referring to one's duty, righteousness, cosmic law, ethics, or inherent nature. Discussed extensively regarding Arjuna's duty as a warrior and societal obligations.
  • Upanishad (उपनिषद्): A class of Sanskrit philosophical texts foundational to Hinduism, often presented as dialogues between teacher and student, exploring concepts like Brahman and Atman. Referenced multiple times.
  • Chandogya Upanishad (छान्दोग्योपनिषद्): One of the major Upanishads. Referenced in relation to the importance of questioning and understanding beyond the senses.
  • Marga (मार्ग): Sanskrit for 'path' or 'way'. Mentioned in the context of the three paths to liberation (*Jnana Marga*, *Bhakti Marga*).
  • Jnana Marga (ज्ञानमार्ग): The path of knowledge or wisdom in Hinduism.
  • Bhakti Marga (भक्तिमार्ग): The path of devotion in Hinduism. Referenced in relation to focusing on Krishna.
  • Karma (कर्म): Sanskrit term for 'action', 'work', or 'deed'. Also refers to the principle of cause and effect. Discussed in the context of acting without attachment to the 'fruits' (results) of action.
  • Katha Upanishad (कठोपनिषद्): An Upanishad featuring a dialogue between Nachiketa and Yama (Death). Discussed by Brad for its chariot allegory (body as chariot, senses as horses, self as rider).
  • Atman (आत्मन्): Sanskrit term for the 'self', 'soul', or eternal essence of an individual. Mentioned in the context of the Katha Upanishad allegory.
  • Brahman (ब्रह्मन्): The ultimate, unchanging reality in Hinduism; the supreme cosmic spirit. Mentioned in relation to the Katha Upanishad and *Brahman Nirvana*.
  • Brahman Nirvana (ब्रह्मनिर्वाण): A term found in the Bhagavad Gita (e.g., 2.72) signifying liberation or extinction (Nirvana) in the ultimate reality (Brahman). Discussed by Brad at the end of the class.
  • Nirvana (निर्वाण): Sanskrit term literally meaning 'to blow out' or 'extinguish'. Commonly associated with Buddhism as the state of liberation from suffering, but also used in Hinduism (as in *Brahman Nirvana*). Explained by Brad.

Key Takeaways

  • Arjuna's dilemma in Chapter 1 highlights the profound conflict between personal relationships/empathy and societal/sacred duty (*dharma*).
  • Krishna's response in Chapter 2 emphasizes the immortality of the soul (*atman*) and the transient nature of the physical body, reframing the act of killing in battle as targeting the impermanent.
  • A central teaching is the importance of performing one's duty (*karma*) diligently but without attachment to the outcomes or 'fruits' of those actions. This resonates with concepts like Kant's categorical imperative.
  • The Bhagavad Gita can be interpreted on multiple levels, including allegorically. The *Katha Upanishad*'s chariot metaphor (body as chariot, senses as horses, intellect as driver, self as passenger) provides a psychological framework for understanding the internal struggle.
  • Mahatma Gandhi drew significant inspiration from the Gita, interpreting its call to action (*karma*) as a mandate for non-violent resistance (*satyagraha*) against injustice, emphasizing the need to act even when not directly harmed.
  • The act of questioning is crucial for spiritual understanding, as demonstrated by Arjuna's inquiries and highlighted by references to the Upanishadic tradition.
  • There are multiple paths (*margas*) to liberation, including the path of knowledge (*jnana*) and the path of devotion (*bhakti*), both referenced in the discussion.
  • The term *Brahman Nirvana* (Gita 2.72) signifies a state of ultimate peace and liberation achieved through union with the absolute reality (*Brahman*), linking Hindu concepts with the idea of *nirvana*.
