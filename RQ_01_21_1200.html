Hindu Theology: Introduction to Shruti, Vedas, and Mimamsa

Synopsis

This lecture introduces foundational concepts for studying Hindu theology. Brad begins with a brief meditation and mantra before diving into key vocabulary, primarily Sanskrit terms. They explain the concept of *Shruti* (revealed scripture) as "that which is heard" by ancient *Rishis*, emphasizing its *Apaurusheya* (non-human origin) nature in many traditions. The structure of the Vedas is outlined, covering the four main recensions (Rig, Sama, Yajur, Atharva) and the four types of texts within each (*Samhita*, *Brahmana*, *Aranyaka*, *Upanishad*). Brad introduces the *Shad Darshana* (six perspectives) of Hindu philosophy, focusing particularly on the *Mimamsa* school. *Mimamsa* is presented as a ritual-centric perspective deeply concerned with the precise interpretation of scripture (*Shruti*) to understand *Dharma* (what is to be done). Key *Mimamsa* principles are discussed, including the idea that scripture dictates duty/action rather than describing reality (which is the domain of perception, *Pratyaksha*), and the concept of *Arthavada* (motivational texts). The lecture concludes by framing the assigned Rigveda creation hymns through the *Mimamsa* lens before transitioning into a class discussion of Rigveda X.129.

Key Takeaways

  • Hindu theology anchors itself in canonical texts, primarily the *Shruti* (Vedas), considered "heard" or directly perceived by *Rishis*, not authored (*Apaurusheya*).
  • The Vedas are divided into four recensions (Rig, Sama, Yajur, Atharva) and four chronological/thematic sections (*Samhita*, *Brahmana*, *Aranyaka*, *Upanishad*).
  • The *Shad Darshana* are six primary perspectives or schools of Hindu philosophy (*Mimamsa*, *Vedanta*, *Nyaya*, *Vaisheshika*, *Samkhya*, *Yoga*).
  • *Mimamsa* focuses intensely on the correct interpretation and performance of Vedic rituals (*Yajnas*).
  • For *Mimamsa*, the existence or non-existence of gods is secondary; the precise performance of the ritual is paramount (*Shabda Matram Devata*).
  • *Mimamsa* views scripture (*Shruti*) not as a source of knowledge about what *is* or *was* (that's for *Pratyaksha* - perception), but solely about what *is to be done* (*Dharma*).
  • Non-injunctive parts of the Veda (like hymns or philosophical sections) are considered *Arthavada* – statements meant to motivate the performance of rituals.
  • Performing rituals correctly brings *Dharma* (righteousness, order, goodness) into being; it's an active creation, not a passive state.
  • Creation hymns, from a *Mimamsa* perspective, should be read as *Arthavada* motivating ritual action, not as historical accounts.
