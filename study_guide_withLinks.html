Comprehensive Vocabulary

Abhava Pramāṇa (अभाव प्रमाण)
Valid source of knowledge through non-existence or absence, particularly relevant to material causality (discussed by Kumārila Bhaṭṭa).
Abhiṣikta (अभिषिक्त)
Sanskrit term for "anointed one," used as a translation for Christos.
Adhikara (अधिकार)
Qualification, authority (esp. to perform rituals).
Aditam (अदितम्)
Things accomplished together.
Adonai (אֲדֹנָי)
Hebrew term meaning "My Lord," used as a substitute for the Tetragrammaton.
Advaita (अद्वैत)
Non-dualism, "not two." A major school of Vedānta philosophy emphasizing the ultimate oneness of reality (Brahman) and the individual self (Ātman). Often considered non-theistic (lacking a personal creator God).
Advaita Vedānta (अद्वैत वेदान्त)
See Advaita.
Aham (अहम्)
I. Discussed as an indexical pronoun pointing to the one universal Self (Ātman).
Aham Brahmāsmi (अहं ब्रह्मास्मि)
"I am Brahman." One of the four Mahāvākyas (Great Sayings) from the Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad.
Aitareya Upaniṣad (ऐतरेय उपनिषद्)
Source of the third Mahāvākya, Prajñānam Brahma.
Akarma (अकर्म)
Inaction. Contrasted with Karma; the Gītā explores seeing action in inaction and inaction in action.
Akṣa (अक्ष)
Eye; root word related to perception in Pratyakṣa.
Amṛtam (अमृतम्)
Immortality, the opposite of death; the fullness of life. From the opening mantra.
Ananta (अनन्त)
Endless, eternal. Used to describe the Vedas in Mīmāṃsā.
Anu (अनु)
After.
Anu Gītā (अनुगीता)
"Gita Again"; a text within the Mahābhārata where Kṛṣṇa gives a condensed reiteration of the Bhagavad Gītā's teachings to Arjuna.
Anumāna (अनुमान)
Logical inference; knowledge derived from prior perception (e.g., inferring fire from smoke). (From *anu* 'after' + *māna* 'thinking').
Anuśloka (अनुश्लोक)
A specific Sanskrit verse meter.
Anuṣṭubh (अनुष्टुभ्)
A common Sanskrit poetic meter, often consisting of four lines (pādas) of eight syllables each, used in texts like the Devī Māhātmyam and Bhagavad Gītā.
Anusvāra (अनुस्वार)
The nasal sound (ṃ) often ending Om.
Anta (अन्त)
End. Used in the explanation of Vedānta (end of the Veda).
Apauruṣeya (अपौरुषेय)
Not of human origin; authorless (referring to the Vedas).
apophatic / Apophasis (ἀπόφασις)
(Greek) Negation or denial, used in theology to speak of God by denying concepts or attributes (corresponds to via negativa).
Āpti (आप्ति)
Obtaining, attainment (related to 'A' of Om).
Āraṇyaka (आरण्यक)
"Forest texts"; part of the Veda containing philosophical reflections on rituals.
Āryan
Term used historically (though potentially controversial) referring to Indo-European groups migrating into India, associated with Sanskrit and traditionally male deities.
Artha (अर्थ)
Goal, aim, purpose, meaning. As one of the Puruṣārthas, it specifically refers to material prosperity, wealth, and worldly success.
Arthāpatti (अर्थापत्ति)
Postulation or presumption; deriving knowledge from circumstances that need explanation.
Arthavada (अर्थवाद)
Explanatory or motivating statements in scripture (often contrasted with injunctions), aimed at encouraging ritual action.
Arjuna (अर्जुन)
One of the five Pāṇḍava brothers, a great warrior, and the central human figure in the Bhagavad Gītā to whom Kṛṣṇa imparts his teachings.
Asat (असत्)
Non-being, untruth. The opposite of Sat. From the opening mantra.
Āśrama (आश्रम)
Stages of life in Hinduism (Brahmacarya, Gṛhastha, Vānaprastha, Sannyāsa).
Asura (असुर)
Demon, anti-god, powerful being often in conflict with the Devas (Suras).
Astika (आस्तिक)
Literally "it is" or "one who says 'it is'". Refers to schools of Indian philosophy that accept the authority of the Vedas (e.g., the six Darśanas).
Atharva Veda (अथर्व वेद)
The fourth Veda, containing hymns, spells, and incantations.
Ātman (आत्मन्)
The Self, soul; the individual essence. In Advaita, identified with Brahman.
Atmos (ἀτμός)
Greek word for vapor, steam; discussed in relation to atmosphere and Atman.
atomist
Relating to the philosophical view that reality is composed of fundamental indivisible units (atoms). Vaiśeṣika is an atomist school.
Avatāra (अवतार)
"Descent." Refers to the appearance or incarnation of a deity (especially Viṣṇu) on Earth in a physical form.
Ayam Ātmā Brahma (अयम् आत्मा ब्रह्म)
"This Self (Ātman) is Brahman," the fourth Mahāvākya, from the Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad (previewed).
Baal (בַּעַל)
A title and honorific meaning "lord" in Northwest Semitic languages; also the name of a major Canaanite and Phoenician deity associated with storms, fertility, and agriculture.
Bala Krishna (बाल कृष्ण)
Krishna in his infant/child form.
Bara (בָּרָא)
Hebrew verb in Genesis 1:1, often translated "created." Rashi's interpretation emphasizes the meaning "to separate" or "to cut," suggesting creation as an act of ordering chaos.
Bereshit (בְּרֵאשִׁית)
The first word of the Hebrew Bible (Genesis 1:1), usually translated "In the beginning." Rashi's interpretation, "When [God] began to create/separate," was discussed.
Bhagavad Gītā (भगवद् गीता / भगवद्गीता)
"Song of the Lord"; a central Hindu scripture (Smṛti) detailing a conversation between Kṛṣṇa and Arjuna, part of the Mahābhārata epic. To be studied extensively.
Bhāgavata Purāṇa (भागवतपुराण)
One of the most important Purāṇas, focusing on devotion (Bhakti) to Viṣṇu, especially in his form as Kṛṣṇa.
Bhagavān (भगवान्)
"Lord," "Blessed One," or "Illustrious One"; often used as a title for Kṛṣṇa or a supreme deity.
Bhakti (भक्ति)
Devotion, love, worship directed towards a personal deity. One of the three paths (Trimārga) to liberation.
Bhakti Mārga (भक्तिमार्ग)
The path of devotion, one of the Trimārga.
Bharata (भरत)
The traditional Sanskrit name for the Indian subcontinent; also the name of an ancient king and the clan featured in the Mahābhārata.
Bhāva (भाव)
Being, existence, state.
bhuj (भुज्)
Sanskrit root meaning "to eat" or "enjoy."
bhunaktu (भुनक्तु)
"Let us be nourished." From the root bhuj.
Bīja (बीज)
Seed (as in Rakta-bīja, blood-seed).
Brahmacarya (ब्रह्मचर्य)
The first stage of life; the student stage focused on learning and discipline, often including celibacy.
Brahman (ब्रह्मन्)
The ultimate reality, the Absolute, the ground of all being in Hinduism.
Brāhmaṇa (ब्राह्मण)
(1) A member of the priestly/scholarly varṇa. (2) A genre of Vedic texts focused on ritual explanation and commentary (plural: Brāhmaṇas). The focus of Mīmāṃsā.
Bṛhad (बृहद्)
Big, great. Part of the name Bṛhadāraṇyaka.
Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad (बृहदारण्यक उपनिषद्)
"Great Forest Teaching/Upaniṣad," one of the oldest and most important Upaniṣads, discussed extensively in class.
Canaanite
Relating to the people, culture, or language of ancient Canaan in the Near East. Used in reference to deities like Baal and Yam and their potential connection to Psalm 104.
Caste
The social stratification system in traditional Hindu society. Arjuna expresses concern about the intermixture of castes resulting from the war.
cataphasis (κατάφασις)
(Greek) Affirmation, used in theology to speak of God using positive descriptions and attributes (corresponds to via positiva).
cataphatic theology
Theological approach describing God through positive affirmations (what God *is*).
Chāndogya Upaniṣad (छान्दोग्य उपनिषद्)
A major Upaniṣad belonging to the Sāma Veda, famous for the teaching "Tat Tvam Asi." Sections 8-16 were discussed in detail.
Christos (Χριστός)
Greek for "anointed one," the title Christ.
Creatio continua
Latin theological term meaning "continuous creation," the concept that God continuously sustains creation. Discussed in relation to Rashi's interpretation and Psalm 104's present-tense descriptions and theme of renewal.
Dakṣa (दक्ष)
Often means "skillful" or "able"; also a creator deity, sometimes identified with Prajāpati or related to Aditi. Mentioned in a translation of Hiraṇyagarbha Sūkta.
Darśana (दर्शन)
Seeing, view, perspective; a school of philosophy.
Dénouement
French term for the final resolution or outcome of a story.
Deva / Devī (देव / देवी)
God / Goddess. Celestial beings. The recipient of a sacrifice.
Devanāgarī (देवनागरी)
The script used for writing Sanskrit and other North Indian languages.
Devata (देवता)
Deity, godhead. (Used in Śabda Matram Devata).
Devī (देवी)
Sanskrit for "Goddess," the divine feminine, worshipped in many forms.
Devī Māhātmya / Devi Mahatmyam (देवीमाहात्म्य / देवी माहात्म्यम्)
"Glory of the Goddess," the primary text discussed in RQ_01_28, part of the Mārkaṇḍeya Purāṇa.
Dharma (धर्म)
Duty, ethics, righteousness, cosmic order, law, virtue, essential nature. A core concept.
Dharmakṣetre Kurukṣetre... (धर्मक्षेत्रे कुरुक्षेत्रे समवेता युयुत्सवः । मामकाः पाण्डवाश्चैव किमकुर्वत सञ्जय ॥)
The opening verse (śloka) of the Bhagavad Gītā.
Dravidian
Peoples and language family primarily in South India, historically associated by some scholars with Śākta traditions.
Drish (दृश्)
Sanskrit root meaning "to see." (Source of Darśana).
Dṛtarāṣṭra (धृतराष्ट्र)
The blind king, father of the Kauravas in the Mahābhārata.
Durgā (दुर्गा)
A major form of the Hindu Goddess, depicted as a warrior goddess slaying Mahiṣāsura.
Dvaita (द्वैत)
Two, dual. The concept negated by Advaita.
Ecclesiastes (קֹהֶלֶת - Qōheleṯ)
A book in the Hebrew Bible (Ketuvim section) and Christian Old Testament.
El (אֵל - ʾĒl)
A Northwest Semitic word meaning "god" or "deity," or referring to the supreme god.
Elohim (אֱלֹהִים)
A Hebrew name for God, grammatically plural but often used with singular verbs. The primary name for God in Genesis 1.
En archē ēn ho logos... (Ἐν ἀρχῇ ἦν ὁ Λόγος, καὶ ὁ Λόγος ἦν πρὸς τὸν Θεόν, καὶ Θεὸς ἦν ὁ Λόγος.)
The opening verse of the Gospel of John in Greek.
Enuma Elish
The Babylonian creation myth.
ēn (ἦν)
Was. Used in Brad's quote of John 1:1.
Eros (Ἔρως)
Greek term for love, particularly passionate or erotic love.
Essenes
A Jewish sect, likely responsible for writing/preserving the Dead Sea Scrolls.
Eucharist
The Christian ceremony commemorating the Last Supper. Mentioned as less emphasized in John's Gospel.
fides quaerens intellectum
(Latin) "Faith seeking understanding." St. Anselm's definition of theology.
Gaia (Γαῖα)
Earth goddess, mentioned as another name/tradition related to Mother Earth/Tiamat concepts.
Gamaya (गमय)
Lead me, guide me, take me. From the opening mantra.
Gāndhāra (गान्धार)
Ancient region/people mentioned in the analogy of the blindfolded man (Chāndogya Upaniṣad Section 14).
Gaṅgā (गंगा)
The Ganges river, personified as a goddess.
Gaṇeśa (गणेश)
The elephant-headed god of beginnings and remover of obstacles.
Garbha (गर्भ)
Embryo, seed, womb. (Used in Hiranyagarbha).
Gaurī (गौरी)
("The White One" or "The Golden One") A benevolent form of Devī.
Genesis (בְּרֵאשִׁית - Bərēʾšīṯ)
The first book of the Hebrew Bible/Old Testament.
Ghana (घन)
Lump, mass, compact. Used in Prajñāna Ghana.
Gopīs (गोपी)
The cowherd women/girls of Vrindavan, devotees of Krishna.
Gospel of John
One of the four canonical gospels in the New Testament.
Graha (ग्रह)
Sanskrit root related to "grasping," "seizing," "accepting."
Greek Orthodox
Branch of Eastern Orthodox Christianity; discussed regarding its historical connection to the Gospel of John.
Grihasta (गृहस्थ)
The householder stage of life.
guru (गुरु)
Teacher, spiritual guide.
Hari Om (हरि ओम्)
A sacred Sanskrit mantra or invocation.
Hashem (הַשֵּׁם)
Hebrew for "The Name." A common way to refer to God in Judaism without pronouncing the Tetragrammaton (YHWH).
Hashamayim (הַשָּׁמַיִם)
Hebrew for "the heavens," noted for its plural ending.
Hebrew
The original language of the Genesis text.
Hermeneutics
The theory and methodology of interpretation, especially of texts.
Hiranya (हिरण्य)
Gold. (Used in Hiranyagarbha).
Hiranyagarbha (हिरण्यगर्भ)
Golden embryo/seed/womb; a concept in creation hymns.
Hiranyakashipu (हिरण्यकशिपु)
The demon king killed by Narasimha.
I am (Greek: ἐγώ εἰμι - egō eimi)
Refers to the significant self-declarations of Jesus in the Gospel of John.
imago dei (Latin)
Image of God. Discussed in relation to Genesis 1:26-27.
Indra (इन्द्र)
An ancient Vedic deity, King of the Devas (gods), associated with thunder and storms.
Indriya (इन्द्रिय)
The senses (seeing, hearing, etc.), related allegorically to Indra.
Isha (ईश)
Lord, ruler; a name for God.
Īśvara (ईश्वर)
Lord, God, often referring to a personal God or creator figure, particularly emphasized in Viśiṣṭādvaita.
Iti (इति)
Thus, so; used stylistically like quotation marks.
Itihāsa (इतिहास)
"History," a category of Hindu texts (Smṛti) including epics.
Itikartavyatavadin (इतिकर्तव्यतावादिन्)
"Those who say 'it should be done this way'"; Nagarjuna's term for Mimamsakas.
Jainism
An ancient Indian religion emphasizing non-violence; mentioned as a Nastika tradition.
Jīva / Jīva Ātman (जीव / जीव आत्मन्)
The living self, the individual soul, particularly as embodied.
Jñāna (ज्ञान)
Knowledge, wisdom, understanding. One of the three paths (mārgas).
Jñāna Mārga (ज्ञानमार्ग / ज्ञान मार्ग)
The path of knowledge or wisdom, emphasized by Shankara and in Vedanta.
JPS Translation
Jewish Publication Society translation of the Hebrew Bible.
Jyotir (ज्योतिर्)
Light. From the opening mantra.
Kāla (काल)
Time; related to the name Kālī.
Kālī (काली)
"The Black One," a fierce form of the Goddess, associated with time and destruction of evil.
Kāliya (कालिय)
A venomous Nāga (serpent) subdued by Krishna.
Kama (काम)
Desire, pleasure, enjoyment, especially sensual or aesthetic pleasure (one of the Purusharthas).
kara (कर)
Sanskrit root related to "making," "doing," "acting"; also means "hand." Related to English "carve."
karavāvahai (करवावहै)
"Let us accomplish/create/carve together."
Karma (कर्म)
Action, work, or deed; also refers to the principle of cause and effect. In Mimamsa, specifically ritual action. One of the three paths (mārgas).
Karma Kāṇḍa (कर्मकाण्ड)
The section of the Vedas dealing with rituals.
Karma Mārga (कर्म मार्ग)
The path of action/ritual, one of the Trimārga.
Karma Yoga (कर्मयोग)
The discipline/path of action, the title and subject of Bhagavad Gītā Chapter 3.
Kartavyata (कर्तव्यता)
Obligation, duty, how something should be done.
Kārtikeya (कार्तिकेय)
God of war, son of Śiva and Pārvatī.
Katha Upanishad (कठोपनिषद्)
An Upanishad featuring a dialogue between Nachiketa and Yama (Death). Discussed for its chariot allegory.
Kaurava (कौरव)
Descendants of King Kuru, specifically the 100 sons of Dhṛtarāṣṭra, the antagonists in the Mahābhārata.
kenosis (Greek: κένωσις)
Emptying, self-emptying. Mentioned in relation to Jesus's divinity in human form.
Kṛṣṇa (कृष्ण)
"Black" or "dark blue." A major Hindu deity, considered the eighth avatar of Viṣṇu, central figure in the Bhagavad Gītā and Bhāgavata Purāṇa.
Kṣatriya (क्षत्रिय)
The second varṇa; warriors, rulers, administrators.
Kumārila Bhaṭṭa (कुमारिल भट्ट)
Influential 7th-8th century Mimāṁsā philosopher, known for his analysis of Pramāṇas, including Abhāva.
Kuru (कुरु)
The name of an ancient king and the dynasty/clan central to the Mahābhārata.
Kurukṣetra (कुरुक्षेत्र)
The field of the Kurus; the location of the great battle in the Mahābhārata.
Kurma (कूर्म)
"Tortoise." The second avatar of Viṣṇu.
Kṣetra (क्षेत्र)
Field.
Leviathan (לִוְיָתָן)
A great sea monster mentioned in the Hebrew Bible.
Līlā (लीला)
Divine play, sport, cosmic drama. A key theme.
Lokasaṅgraha (लोकसंग्रह)
World-maintenance, holding the world/people together, the welfare of all. Presented as a key motivation for detached action in the Gītā.
Logos (λόγος)
Greek for Word, reason, principle. Discussed in relation to Genesis 1, Proverbs 8, and John 1.
lux
Latin for light.
Mā (मा)
Me. From the opening mantra.
Ma vidviṣāvahai (मा विद्विषावहै)
"Let us not hate/be hostile towards each other."
Madhukāṇḍa (मधुकाण्ड)
"Honey Section" - a part of the Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad.
Maha (महा)
Great.
Mahābhārata (महाभारत)
"The Great Epic of the Bharata Dynasty"; one of the two major Sanskrit epics (Itihāsa), containing the Bhagavad Gītā.
Mahāvākya / Mahāvākyāni (महावाक्य / महावाक्यानि)
"Great Saying(s)"; core aphorisms from the Upaniṣads expressing central Vedāntic truths.
Mahīṣa / Mahiṣāsura (महिष / महिषासुर)
The Buffalo Demon slain by Durgā.
Makhan Chor (माखन चोर)
"Butter Thief," a playful epithet for the child Krishna.
Māna (मान)
Thinking, measuring.
Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad (माण्डूक्य उपनिषद्)
A short Upaniṣad focusing on states of consciousness and Om; source of "Ayam Ātmā Brahma".
Mantra (मन्त्र)
Sacred utterance, syllable, word, or group of words believed to have psychological and spiritual power.
Marduk
Chief god of Babylon in the Enuma Elish creation myth.
Mārga (मार्ग)
Path, way, often referring to paths of liberation.
Mārkaṇḍeya Purāṇa (मार्कण्डेय पुराण)
One of the major Purāṇas, containing the Devī Māhātmyam.
Matram (मात्रं)
Only, merely, nothing but.
Matsya (मत्स्य)
"Fish." The first avatar of Viṣṇu.
Medhas (मेधस्)
Intellect, wisdom. A character in the Devī Māhātmyam frame story.
Mela (मेला)
A fair or gathering, often religious.
Mīmāṃsā (मीमांसा)
One of the six orthodox schools (Ṣaḍ Darśana), focused on the exegesis of the Vedas, emphasizing ritual duty (*karma*). Sister school to Vedānta.
Mimamsaka
Follower of the Mimamsa school.
Miti (मिति)
Measuring (related to 'M' of Om).
Mokṣa (मोक्ष)
Liberation, release (from ignorance, from the cycle of rebirth). The ultimate Purushartha.

Consolidated Key Takeaways & Themes

  • Course Focus: Comparative study of Hindu (Śruti/Smṛti) and Christian (Hebrew/Greek) sacred texts, examining creation, ethics (Dharma), divine play (Līlā), and non-dualism (Advaita).
  • Theological Method: Understanding theology as *fides quaerens intellectum* (faith seeking understanding), involving a quest and questioning assumptions. Using the *Saṅgraha-Vistara* method (condensation and elaboration) for textual study.
  • Hindu Foundations: Understanding the structure of the Vedas (Saṃhitā, Brāhmaṇa, Āraṇyaka, Upaniṣad), the concept of Śruti (revealed scripture), the Ṣaḍ Darśanas (six orthodox schools), Purusharthas (aims of life), Ashramas (stages), Varnas (societal divisions).
  • Mīmāṃsā Philosophy: Focus on ritual action (karma/yajña) derived from Vedic injunctions; scripture's primary role is prescriptive (what is to be done - Dharma), not descriptive of reality.
  • Advaita Vedānta: Core teaching of non-duality (Ātman = Brahman) explored through Mahāvākyas like "Aham Brahmāsmi" and "Tat Tvam Asi." Emphasizes knowledge (Jñāna Mārga) for liberation from ignorance. Creation as unfolding (Śṛṣṭi).
  • Viśiṣṭādvaita Vedānta: Qualified non-dualism, incorporating a personal God (Īśvara) within the ultimate unity.
  • Creation Narratives: Analysis of ṚgVeda hymns (Nāsadīya Sūkta, Hiraṇyagarbha Sūkta, Puruṣa Sūkta), Genesis 1-3, Proverbs 8-9. Examining themes of origin (from non-being, a golden embryo, sacrifice), questioning, the role of wisdom/speech/breath, order vs. chaos, and the nature of the creator(s).
  • Devī Māhātmyam & Śāktism: Understanding the Goddess (Devī) as Śakti (divine power), interpreting the text allegorically (characters as body/mind/spirit, demons as inner obstacles), and exploring the three guṇas (Tamas, Rajas, Sattva).
  • Bhagavad Gītā & John's Gospel: Introduced as key texts for the latter half of the course, exploring Arjuna's dilemma, Krishna's teachings (duty, detachment, nature of self), and John's high Christology and themes of knowing/believing, signs, and the Word (Logos).
  • Key Concepts: *Līlā* (divine play), *Dharma* (duty/ethics), *Karma* (action/ritual), *Mokṣa* (liberation), *Avatāra* (divine descent), *Pramāṇas* (sources of knowledge, including *Abhāva*), *Causality*, *Brahman/Ātman*.
  • Comparative Insights: Noting parallels and divergences between traditions regarding creation, the nature of the divine, human purpose, and paths to salvation/liberation. Understanding historical contexts (e.g., Genesis vs. Enuma Elish).
  • Class Structure: Emphasis on textual analysis, discussion, team-based learning (podcasts), and minimizing distractions (no-screen policy).
