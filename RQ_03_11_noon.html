Synopsis

This class session began with administrative details, including reminders about student meetings, the upcoming no-screen policy (requiring printed texts starting Thursday), and the structure for the first all-class discussion leader day. Brad then transitioned into a review and synthesis of key concepts covered so far, emphasizing the distinction between theology (as faith seeking understanding anchored in a canon) and the general study of religion. They revisited the six orthodox Hindu traditions, focusing on Mimamsa (ritual exegesis) and Vedanta (Upanishadic philosophy). The core concepts of Advaita (non-duality) and Vishishtadvaita (qualified non-duality with God) were explored using various metaphors (coin, team/players) and linked to different understandings of causality (material, efficient, final), incorporating Kumārila Bhaṭṭa's concept of *Abhava Pramāṇa*. The second half of the class introduced the two primary texts for the remainder of the semester: the *Bhagavad Gītā* and the Gospel of John. Brad provided context for the *Gītā* within the *Mahābhārata*, outlining the conflict between the Pāṇḍavas and Kauravas, Arjuna's central dilemma, and Kṛṣṇa's role. They also discussed the likely authorship, historical context (late 1st century CE), philosophical style, and key characteristics of the Gospel of John.

Vocabulary

  • Abhava Pramāṇa (अभाव प्रमाण): Valid source of knowledge through non-existence or absence, particularly relevant to material causality (discussed by Kumārila Bhaṭṭa).
  • Advaita (अद्वैत): "Non-duality"; a major school of Vedanta philosophy emphasizing the ultimate oneness of reality (*Brahman*) and the individual self (*ātman*).
  • Anu Gītā (अनुगीता): "Gita Again"; a text within the Mahābhārata where Kṛṣṇa gives a condensed reiteration of the Bhagavad Gītā's teachings to Arjuna.
  • Arjuna (अर्जुन): One of the five Pāṇḍava brothers, a great warrior, and the central human figure in the Bhagavad Gītā to whom Kṛṣṇa imparts his teachings.
  • artha (अर्थ): Purpose, goal, meaning, aim (mentioned alongside Līlā as a potential final cause/telos).
  • Bhagavad Gītā (भगवद् गीता): "Song of the Lord"; a 700-verse Hindu scripture in Sanskrit that is part of the epic *Mahābhārata*.
  • Bhagavān (भगवान्): "Lord," "Blessed One," or "Illustrious One"; often used as a title for Kṛṣṇa or a supreme deity.
  • Bharata (भरत): The traditional Sanskrit name for the Indian subcontinent; also the name of an ancient king and the clan featured in the *Mahābhārata*.
  • Brahman (ब्रह्मन्): The ultimate, unchanging reality in Hinduism; the supreme cosmic spirit. Discussed extensively in the Upanishads and Vedanta.
  • Chandogya Upaniṣad (छान्दोग्योपनिषद्): One of the oldest and most important Upanishads, known for teachings on *Brahman* and the phrase *Tat Tvam Asi*.
  • Dharma (धर्म): A complex concept encompassing duty, righteousness, cosmic law, ethics, inherent nature, and social order.
  • Dharmakṣetre Kurukṣetre... (धर्मक्षेत्रे कुरुक्षेत्रे समवेता युयुत्सवः । मामकाः पाण्डवाश्चैव किमकुर्वत सञ्जय ॥): The opening verse (śloka) of the Bhagavad Gītā.
  • Dṛtarāṣṭra (धृतराष्ट्र): The blind king, father of the Kauravas in the *Mahābhārata*.
  • En archē ēn ho logos... (Ἐν ἀρχῇ ἦν ὁ Λόγος, καὶ ὁ Λόγος ἦν πρὸς τὸν Θεόν, καὶ Θεὸς ἦν ὁ Λόγος.): The opening verse of the Gospel of John in Greek, meaning "In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God."
  • Gītā (गीता): Song.
  • karma (कर्म): Action, work, or deed; also refers to the principle of causality where intent and actions influence the future. In the Mimamsa context discussed, it specifically refers to ritual action.
  • Kaurava (कौरव): Descendants of King Kuru, specifically referring to the 100 sons of Dṛtarāṣṭra, the antagonists in the *Mahābhārata*. (Referred to as 'Kavas' in transcript).
  • Kṛṣṇa (कृष्ण): A major deity in Hinduism, considered an avatar of Vishnu; serves as Arjuna's charioteer and divine guide in the *Bhagavad Gītā*.
  • Kuru (कुरु): The name of an ancient king and the dynasty/clan central to the *Mahābhārata*.
  • Kumārila Bhaṭṭa (कुमारिल भट्ट): An influential philosopher of the Mimamsa school of Hindu philosophy (c. 7th century CE).
  • Līlā (लीला): "Play," "sport"; a concept in Hinduism describing the cosmic activity of the divine as playful and spontaneous, not driven by need.
  • logos (λόγος): A Greek term meaning "word," "reason," "principle," or "discourse." Used philosophically and theologically, notably in the prologue of the Gospel of John to refer to the divine Word (Jesus Christ).
  • Mahābhārata (महाभारत): "The Great Epic of the Bharata Dynasty"; one of the two major Sanskrit epics of ancient India (the other being the Rāmāyaṇa), containing the *Bhagavad Gītā*.
  • Mimamsa (मीमांसा): "Inquiry" or "Investigation"; one of the six orthodox (āstika) schools of Hindu philosophy, focused on the exegesis of the Vedas, particularly the Samhitas and Brahmanas, to ascertain Dharma as ritual duty.
  • Pāṇḍava (पाण्डव): The five sons of King Pāṇḍu (Yudhishthira, Bhima, Arjuna, Nakula, Sahadeva), the protagonists of the *Mahābhārata*.
  • Pāṇḍu (पाण्डु): The father of the Pāṇḍavas in the *Mahābhārata*.
  • Pramāṇa (प्रमाण): A valid means or source of knowledge in Indian philosophy.
  • Quid est veritas?: Latin for "What is truth?", the question Pontius Pilate asks Jesus in the Gospel of John (John 18:38).
  • rāja (राज): King, ruler.
  • śloka (श्लोक): A specific Sanskrit poetic meter, typically a couplet of two 16-syllable lines or four 8-syllable lines, commonly used in the epics and the *Gītā*.
  • Tat Tvam Asi (तत् त्वम् असि): "That thou art" or "You are that"; a famous Mahāvākya (Great Saying) from the *Chandogya Upaniṣad*, expressing the identity of the individual self (*ātman*) with the ultimate reality (*Brahman*). Central to Advaita Vedanta.
  • Theos (Θεός): The Greek word for God.
  • Upanishad (उपनिषद्): "Sitting near devotedly"; a collection of late Vedic Sanskrit texts that form the philosophical foundation of Hinduism. They are considered part of the Vedas and represent Vedanta ("end of the Veda").
  • Veda (वेद): "Knowledge"; the oldest body of Sanskrit literature and the foundational scriptures of Hinduism, considered *śruti* (that which is heard, revealed). They are the canonical anchor for the orthodox schools.
  • Vedanta (वेदान्त): "End of the Veda"; one of the six orthodox (āstika) schools of Hindu philosophy, focusing primarily on the philosophical teachings of the Upanishads.
  • Vishishtadvaita (विशिष्टाद्वैत): "Qualified Non-duality"; a prominent school of Vedanta philosophy founded by Ramanuja, asserting that the ultimate reality (*Brahman*) is non-dual but possesses real attributes and distinctions, including individual souls and matter, which form its 'body'.
  • yajña (यज्ञ): Sacrifice, worship, offering; a central ritual practice in Vedic religion, emphasized by the Mimamsa school.

Key Takeaways

  • This course studies theology, defined as a philosophical investigation of first principles anchored in a canon of texts (or "faith seeking understanding"), rather than religion in a broader sense.
  • Vedanta and Mimamsa are two of the six orthodox Hindu theological schools, meaning they accept the Vedas as revealed scripture.
  • Mimamsa focuses on Vedic exegesis for understanding ritual duties (*yajña*).
  • Vedanta ("end of the Veda") applies hermeneutics to the Upanishads to understand ultimate reality (*Brahman*).
  • Advaita Vedanta teaches non-duality: ultimate reality (*Brahman*) and the individual self are not two separate things. Metaphors include the two sides of a coin, the universal and particular, or a team functioning as one.
  • Vishishtadvaita Vedanta teaches qualified non-duality, incorporating a personal God who is the ultimate reality containing all diversity within itself.
  • Causality (material, efficient, final) provides a framework for comparing these schools. In Vishishtadvaita, God is the efficient cause shaping reality. In Advaita, the efficient cause is more complex, involving both the self and the world shaping each other.
  • *Abhava Pramāṇa* (knowledge from absence), a Mimamsa concept, helps understand material cause: the cause (milk) ceases to exist at the moment the effect (yogurt) comes into being.
  • The *Bhagavad Gītā* is set within the *Mahābhārata* epic, depicting a conversation between the warrior Arjuna and his charioteer Kṛṣṇa (an avatar of God) on the brink of a devastating civil war. Arjuna faces a moral crisis about fighting his own kin.
  • The Gospel of John, written in sophisticated Greek likely in the late 1st century CE (possibly dictated by an eyewitness like the "beloved disciple"), presents a highly theological and philosophical portrayal of Jesus, emphasizing his divinity and using concepts like *logos*.
  • Both the *Gītā* and John's Gospel emerged around the 1st century CE but represent distinct theological traditions anchored in different canons (Vedas vs. Hebrew Bible/emerging Christian texts).
