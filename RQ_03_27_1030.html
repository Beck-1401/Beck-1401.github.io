Gospel of John (Chapters 10-13) Class Discussion

Synopsis

The class began slightly late, followed by a brief moment of presence and breathing led by Brad. Brad offered the class options for an opening activity: playing Jenga or revisiting Jeopardy questions. The class, particularly the group leaders, voted for Jenga. A significant portion of the initial class time was dedicated to playing Jenga, involving group leaders and other students. Following the game, the class transitioned into a discussion focused on the Gospel of John, specifically chapters 10 through 13. Students raised questions and shared interpretations regarding Jesus's analogies (the shepherd and the gate), the nature of belief versus seeing signs, the timing of salvation (before and after Jesus), Jesus's humanity and relatability (e.g., weeping), apparent contradictions in his statements (judging vs. saving), and the role of the "beloved disciple." Comparisons were drawn to the Bhagavad Gita regarding the transmission of teachings and the need for structure in religion. The discussion also touched upon blind faith versus questioning faith, and the importance of community in religious experience, exemplified by a student's experience with Greek Orthodox services. Brad concluded the class by clarifying the requirements for the upcoming podcast assignment, emphasizing the rotation of text focus between the Bhagavad Gita and the Gospel of John based on group leadership roles.

Vocabulary

  • Bhagavad Gītā (भगवद् गीता): (approx. 27:40, 38:40) A major Hindu scripture, discussed in comparison to the Gospel of John regarding the transmission of divine teachings and the potential for the message to degrade over time, requiring divine intervention (like Krishna's) to reset it. Also mentioned in the context of the podcast assignment rotation.
  • Greek Orthodox: (approx. 33:30) Mentioned by a student describing their religious background and the experience of community and feeling connected during services conducted in Greek, even without understanding the language. Brad noted the deep historical and theological connection between the Greek Orthodox Church and the Gospel of John.

Key Takeaways

  • Jesus's metaphors in John 10 (Shepherd, Gate) raise complex questions about exclusivity, salvation for those before him, and his dual roles.
  • The Gospel of John emphasizes belief, but students questioned how faith is cultivated without Jesus's physical presence and signs, contrasting it with the disbelief of some who witnessed miracles.
  • Jesus's depicted humanity (e.g., weeping in John 11) makes him relatable and trustworthy for some, while others in the text struggled to reconcile his humanity with his claims of divinity.
  • Apparent contradictions within the text (e.g., Jesus coming to judge vs. not coming to judge) provoke discussion about interpretation and the multifaceted nature of his role.
  • The need for religious structure and guidance was discussed as a human desire, contrasting with the simple call to "be nice."
  • Faith can involve both intellectual seeking/questioning (theology as "faith seeking understanding") and experiential connection through community and ritual, even without full linguistic comprehension (Greek Orthodox example).
  • The figure of the "disciple whom Jesus loved" is introduced in John 13, an important recurring element in this Gospel.
  • There's a tension explored between internal belief/love/intention and the necessity of backing up faith with action (highlighted by Peter's impending denial).
