Synopsis

This class session began with attendance and a brief centering meditation. Brad reviewed student podcast submissions and outlined upcoming class logistics, emphasizing the shift to a discussion-based format without screens starting Thursday, requiring printed texts. The core of the session was a detailed review of key concepts from Hindu philosophy, specifically contrasting the Mīmāṃsā and Vedānta schools (Ṣaḍ Darśana). Brad elaborated on Mīmāṃsā's focus on ritual (karma, yajña) and Abhāva Pramāṇa (non-being as knowledge source) via Kumārila Bhaṭṭa. They then revisited the two Vedānta sub-schools studied: Advaita (non-dualism, exploring concepts like Brahman/particular, matter/energy, team/players, brain function) and Viśiṣṭādvaita (qualified non-dualism, incorporating Īśvara/God). The discussion included different types of causality (material, efficient, final/telos/artha) and the concept of Līlā (divine play) as the purpose of existence. Student questions addressed Aristotle's formal cause and free will within Viśiṣṭādvaita. Finally, Brad introduced the two texts for the next phase of the course: the Bhagavad Gītā and the Gospel of John. They provided historical context (1st century CE origins), authorship questions, linguistic backgrounds (Sanskrit/Greek), and structural overviews. For the Gītā, the context within the Mahābhārata (Kauravas vs. Pāṇḍavas, Arjuna's despondency) was explained, highlighting Chapter 2's density. For John's Gospel, theories about the "beloved disciple" and its sophisticated theological nature were discussed, along with the significance of Passover. The session concluded with reading assignments for the Gospel of John.

Vocabulary

  • Ṣaḍ Darśana (षड् दर्शन): The six orthodox perspectives or schools of thought in Hindu philosophy, all accepting the authority of the Vedas.
  • Vedānta (वेदान्त): One of the Ṣaḍ Darśana; literally "end of the Vedas," focusing on the Upaniṣads and applying Mīmāṃsā interpretive rules. Includes sub-schools like Advaita and Viśiṣṭādvaita.
  • Mīmāṃsā (मीमांसा): One of the Ṣaḍ Darśana, sister school to Vedānta, focused on the ritualistic portions (karmas, yajñas) of the Vedas and establishing rules of textual interpretation (exegesis).
  • Karma (कर्म): Action, specifically ritual action or sacrifice in the context of Mīmāṃsā.
  • Yajña (यज्ञ): Ritual sacrifice, central to Mīmāṃsā practice.
  • Kumārila Bhaṭṭa (कुमारिल भट्ट): An influential Mīmāṃsā philosopher known for his views on pramāṇas, including Abhāva Pramāṇa.
  • Pramāṇa (प्रमाण): A valid means or source of knowledge.
  • Abhāva Pramāṇa (अभाव प्रमाण): Non-existence or absence as a valid source of knowledge, used by Kumārila Bhaṭṭa to understand the relationship between material cause and effect (e.g., milk ceasing to exist for curd to arise).
  • Advaita (अद्वैत): Non-dualism; a school of Vedānta asserting the ultimate oneness of reality (Brahman) and the individual self (Ātman), denying fundamental duality between concepts like universal/particular, matter/energy, one/many.
  • Viśiṣṭādvaita (विशिष्टाद्वैत): Qualified non-dualism; a school of Vedānta that posits non-duality but allows for real distinctions within the ultimate reality, particularly affirming a personal God (Īśvara) with attributes who relates to the world and individual souls.
  • Brahman (ब्रह्मन्): The ultimate reality, the Absolute, the ground of all being in Vedānta.
  • Līlā (लीला): Divine play or sport; the concept that creation and existence are a spontaneous, joyful expression of the divine, rather than arising from necessity or lack. Presented as the final cause (Artha/Telos) of existence.
  • Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad (बृहदारण्यक उपनिषद्): One of the oldest and most important Upaniṣads, cited as a source for the idea that the One desired multiplicity out of loneliness or boredom (leading to Līlā).
  • Artha (अर्थ): Goal, purpose, aim, meaning, value. Discussed as the final cause, equivalent to the Greek Telos.
  • Telos (τέλος): Greek term for end, purpose, or goal; the final cause in Aristotelian philosophy.
  • Īśvara (ईश्वर): Lord, God, often referring to a personal God or creator figure, particularly emphasized in Viśiṣṭādvaita as the efficient cause shaping Brahman.
  • Morphē (μορφή): Greek term for form or shape, mentioned in the context of Aristotle's hylomorphism (matter-form theory) when discussing formal cause.
  • Bhagavad Gītā (भगवद् गीता): "Song of the Lord"; a central sacred text in Hinduism, part of the Mahābhārata, presented as a dialogue between Kṛṣṇa and Arjuna on the battlefield.
  • Mahābhārata (महाभारत): One of the two major Sanskrit epics of ancient India (the other being the Rāmāyaṇa), a vast narrative encompassing mythology, philosophy, and the Kurukṣetra War.
  • Bharata (भारत): The traditional Sanskrit name for India or the Indian subcontinent, derived from the legendary king Bharata; also refers to the lineage central to the Mahābhārata.
  • Kauravas (कौरव): The descendants of King Kuru, specifically referring to the 100 sons of Dhṛtarāṣṭra in the Mahābhārata, who oppose the Pāṇḍavas.
  • Pāṇḍavas (पाण्डव): The five sons of King Pāṇḍu in the Mahābhārata (Yudhiṣṭhira, Bhīma, Arjuna, Nakula, Sahadeva), the protagonists who fight against the Kauravas.
  • Kuru (कुरु): The ancient patriarch of the dynasty featured in the Mahābhārata.
  • Dhṛtarāṣṭra (धृतराष्ट्र): The blind king, father of the Kauravas in the Mahābhārata.
  • Pāṇḍu (पाण्डु): Brother of Dhṛtarāṣṭra and father of the Pāṇḍavas in the Mahābhārata.
  • Arjuna (अर्जुन): One of the five Pāṇḍava brothers, a master archer, to whom Kṛṣṇa delivers the teachings of the Bhagavad Gītā.
  • Rāja (राज): King.
  • Kṛṣṇa (कृष्ण): An avatar of the god Viṣṇu, Arjuna's charioteer and divine teacher in the Bhagavad Gītā.
  • Saṃgraha (संग्रह): Condensation, summarization, bringing together. Mentioned in relation to Vistāra.
  • Vistāra (विस्तार): Elaboration, expansion, detailed explanation. Mentioned in relation to Saṃgraha.
  • Dharmakṣetra (धर्मक्षेत्र): Field of Dharma (duty, righteousness, cosmic order). The first word of the Bhagavad Gītā, referring to the battlefield Kurukṣetra.
  • Kurukṣetra (कुरुक्षेत्र): The field of the Kurus; the location of the great battle in the Mahābhārata.
  • Kṣetra (क्षेत्र): Field.
  • Samavetā Yuyutsavaḥ (समवेता युयुत्सवः): Assembled together, desiring to fight (from Bhagavad Gītā 1.1).
  • Māmakāḥ Pāṇḍavāścaiva Kim akurvata Sañjaya (मामकाः पाण्डवाश्चैव किमकुर्वत सञ्जय): "My people (the Kauravas) and the sons of Pāṇḍu—what did they do, O Sañjaya?" (The opening question from Dhṛtarāṣṭra in Bhagavad Gītā 1.1).
  • Sañjaya (सञ्जय): Dhṛtarāṣṭra's charioteer and advisor in the Mahābhārata, granted divine sight to witness the battle and narrate it to the blind king.

Key Takeaways

  • The Ṣaḍ Darśana represent six orthodox viewpoints within Hindu philosophy, all grounded in the Vedas. Mīmāṃsā focuses on ritual and interpretation, while Vedānta focuses on the philosophical teachings of the Upaniṣads.
  • Abhāva Pramāṇa (knowledge from absence) is a key Mīmāṃsā concept explaining how effects arise from the cessation of their material causes.
  • Advaita Vedānta posits radical non-dualism (reality is One/Brahman), while Viśiṣṭādvaita Vedānta posits qualified non-dualism, acknowledging distinctions and a personal God (Īśvara) within the ultimate unity.
  • Understanding different types of causality (material, efficient, final) helps distinguish these schools. Līlā (divine play) is often presented as the final cause or purpose (Artha/Telos) of existence.
  • Viśiṣṭādvaita allows for free will alongside divine agency, suggesting God grants freedom, while Advaita emphasizes the interplay of self-shaping and being shaped by the world (which is also Self).
  • The Bhagavad Gītā, set within the Mahābhārata epic, addresses Arjuna's ethical dilemma on the brink of a civil war against corrupt relatives, prompting Kṛṣṇa's teachings on duty, reality, and devotion. Chapter 2 is particularly dense and foundational.
  • The Gospel of John, written in sophisticated Greek likely around 90-95 CE, presents a highly theological portrayal of Jesus, possibly based on the testimony of an eyewitness disciple ("the beloved disciple") compiled by a later author/editor.
  • Both the Bhagavad Gītā and the Gospel of John emerged in the 1st century CE and explore profound questions of divinity, human purpose, and ethical action within their respective traditions.
  • The class format will shift towards active discussion, requiring students to come prepared with printed texts and engage without screens.
