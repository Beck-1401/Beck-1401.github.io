Vocabulary

  • Advaita Vedānta - Non-dualistic school of Vedānta philosophy, emphasizing the ultimate oneness of Ātman and Brahman. Discussed as the recent unit of study, often considered non-theistic (in terms of a personal God).
  • Mīmāṃsā - One of the six orthodox schools (Ṣaḍ Darśana), focused on the exegesis of the Vedas, particularly the ritualistic portions (Karma-kāṇḍa). Contrasted with Vedānta.
  • Vedānta - One of the six orthodox schools (Ṣaḍ Darśana), focusing on the philosophical teachings of the Upaniṣads (Jñāna-kāṇḍa). Includes Advaita and Viśiṣṭādvaita branches.
  • Viśiṣṭādvaita Vedānta - Qualified non-dualism school of Vedānta, associated with Rāmānuja. Posits Brahman as the supreme reality but allows for real distinctions (qualifications) within Brahman, including individual souls and matter. Considered theistic.
  • Bhagavad Gītā (भगवद् गीता) - "Song of the Lord," an important Hindu scripture, part of the Mahābhārata epic. To be studied extensively after spring break.
  • karma (कर्म) - Action, work, or deed; also refers to the principle of cause and effect. Mentioned as a term Stoler Miller translates, but perhaps shouldn't be.
  • dharma (धर्म) - Duty, righteousness, cosmic law, ethics. Mentioned similarly to karma.
  • Ātman (आत्मन्) - The individual self or soul. Mentioned similarly to karma.
  • Brahman (ब्रह्मन्) - The ultimate reality, the Absolute in Hinduism. Mentioned similarly to karma.
  • pāda (पाद) - Foot, quarter, or section, often referring to the four parts of a verse (*śloka*). Mentioned in relation to Stoler Miller's translation preserving poetic structure.
  • śloka (श्लोक) - A common verse form in Sanskrit poetry, typically consisting of four *pādas*.
  • yoga (योग) - Union, discipline, path. Mentioned as a term Schweig is reluctant to translate; also explained as meaning "joined" or "yoke" in the context of Sāṃkhya.
  • Gospel of John - One of the four canonical gospels in the New Testament. To be studied alongside the Bhagavad Gītā.
  • NRSV - New Revised Standard Version, a modern English translation of the Bible. Recommended version for the Gospel of John study (specifically the Oxford Annotated edition).
  • Ṣaḍ Darśana (षड्दर्शन) - The six orthodox schools (perspectives) of Hindu philosophy: Nyāya, Vaiśeṣika, Sāṃkhya, Yoga, Mīmāṃsā, Vedānta.
  • Nyāya (न्याय) - School of logic and epistemology. Paired with Vaiśeṣika.
  • Vaiśeṣika (वैशेषिक) - School of atomistic metaphysics. Paired with Nyāya. Mentioned as generally theistic and related to atomism.
  • Sāṃkhya (सांख्य) - School emphasizing the dualism between Puruṣa (consciousness/spirit) and Prakṛti (matter/nature). Paired with Yoga. Described as non-theistic and viewing matter/energy as ontologically distinct.
  • Prakṛti (प्रकृति) - Matter, nature, the material world in Sāṃkhya philosophy.
  • Puruṣa (पुरुष) - Consciousness, spirit, the self in Sāṃkhya philosophy; also translated as "person" or "energy."
  • ontological - Relating to the branch of metaphysics dealing with the nature of being (ontos). Used to describe fundamental difference in being (ontologically distinct).
  • ontic - Relating to entities and the facts about them; relating to existence or reality. Used to describe distinctions within a single being or entity (ontic distinctions, ontic union).
  • yoke - A wooden bar joining two draft animals; used as an analogy for the meaning of "yoga" and how Sāṃkhya views the joining of distinct entities (Prakṛti and Puruṣa).
  • atomist - Relating to the philosophical view that reality is composed of fundamental indivisible units (atoms). Vaiśeṣika is an atomist school. Discussed in relation to student questions.
  • non-dualist - Relating to the philosophical view that reality is ultimately one, without fundamental divisions. Advaita Vedānta is a non-dualist school. Contrasted with atomism and Sāṃkhya's dualism.

Key Takeaways

  • A thorough understanding of Advaita Vedānta is crucial before progressing to Viśiṣṭādvaita Vedānta and later course material.
  • Podcast 3 is cancelled; an in-class midterm exam will take its place next Thursday, covering similar content to the quizzes. A study guide will be provided.
  • Podcast assignments should prioritize close textual interpretation and analysis of the assigned sacred texts.
  • For the second half of the course, consider acquiring B. Stoler Miller's or G. Schweig's translation of the *Bhagavad Gītā* and the NRSV Oxford Annotated *Gospel of John* (though PDFs/online versions will be available).
  • The Ṣaḍ Darśana (six schools) offer different perspectives on reality. Sāṃkhya and Yoga posit that matter (Prakṛti) and energy/spirit (Puruṣa) are fundamentally distinct (ontologically separate) but can be temporarily joined (ontic union).
  • Ontological differences relate to fundamental being (e.g., two separate pieces of paper), while ontic differences relate to distinctions within a single being (e.g., quadrants on one folded paper, or heads/tails of one coin).
  • Advaita Vedānta posits ontological oneness (non-dualism), while schools like Sāṃkhya posit ontological dualism (between Prakṛti and Puruṣa). Atomistic schools like Vaiśeṣika posit ontological plurality (many distinct atoms).
